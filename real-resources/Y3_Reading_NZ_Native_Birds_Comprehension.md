# Year 3 Reading: New Zealand Native Birds Comprehension

**New Zealand Curriculum Level 2-3**

## Learning Objectives

Students will be able to:

- Read and understand non-fiction texts about New Zealand birds
- Use comprehension strategies to find information
- Make connections between text and their own experiences
- Develop vocabulary related to New Zealand wildlife

---

## Text: "Amazing New Zealand Birds"

### Passage 1: The Kiwi - New Zealand's Special Bird

The kiwi is New Zealand's most famous bird, but you might never see one! Kiwis are nocturnal, which means they come out at night when you are sleeping.

Kiwis are very unusual birds. They cannot fly, even though they have wings. Their wings are tiny - only about 3 centimeters long. Instead of flying, kiwis use their strong legs to run fast through the forest.

A kiwi's beak is very special. It is long and thin with nostrils at the tip. This helps the kiwi smell worms and insects in the ground. Most birds can't smell very well, but kiwis have an excellent sense of smell!

Baby kiwis are called chicks. When a kiwi chick hatches from its egg, it already has feathers and can walk around. The chick doesn't need its parents to feed it - it can find its own food right away!

Unfortunately, there are not many kiwis left in New Zealand. Dogs, cats, and other animals that came from other countries hunt kiwis. People are working hard to protect these special birds so they don't disappear forever.

### Comprehension Questions - Passage 1

**Literal Questions (Finding information directly in the text):**

1. When do kiwis come out - during the day or at night?
2. How long are a kiwi's wings?
3. What do kiwis use their beaks to find?
4. What are baby kiwis called?

**Inferential Questions (Reading between the lines):** 5. Why might you never see a kiwi in the wild? 6. Why do you think kiwis need strong legs? 7. How is a kiwi chick different from other baby birds you know about?

**Personal Connection Questions:** 8. Have you ever seen a kiwi? Where? 9. What other New Zealand animals do you know about? 10. Why do you think it's important to protect kiwis?

---

### Passage 2: The Tui - A Musical Bird

Have you ever heard a tui singing? Tuis are amazing singers that can make many different sounds. They can whistle, click, grunt, and even copy other birds' songs. Some tuis learn to copy human voices too!

You can recognize a tui by the white feathers on its throat. These white feathers look like a collar or necklace. Tuis also have beautiful green and blue feathers that shine in the sunlight.

Tuis love to drink nectar from flowers. They have a special brush-like tongue that helps them get nectar from deep inside flowers. When tuis visit flowers to drink nectar, they help the plants too. Pollen sticks to their feathers and gets carried to other flowers.

In Māori culture, the tui is very special. There are many stories and songs about tuis. The tui represents the connection between the earth and the sky.

Tuis are very intelligent birds. They can solve problems and use tools. Scientists have watched tuis figure out how to get food from special feeders that other birds can't use.

You might see tuis in your garden if you have the right flowers. They love flax, kowhai, and pohutukawa flowers. If you want to attract tuis, ask your family to plant native New Zealand flowers.

### Comprehension Questions - Passage 2

**Literal Questions:**

1. What do the white feathers on a tui's throat look like?
2. What do tuis like to drink?
3. What type of tongue do tuis have?
4. Name two flowers that tuis love to visit.

**Inferential Questions:** 5. Why might a tui copy other sounds? 6. How do tuis help flowers when they drink nectar? 7. What does it mean that tuis are "intelligent birds"?

**Making Connections:** 8. Have you heard a tui singing? Describe the sound. 9. What flowers do you have in your garden that might attract tuis? 10. Why do you think tuis are special in Māori culture?

---

### Passage 3: The Weka - A Curious Ground Bird

The weka is a brown bird about the size of a chicken. Like the kiwi, wekas cannot fly. They live on the ground and are very good at running and swimming.

Wekas are extremely curious birds. They love to explore and investigate anything new. If you go camping, a weka might visit your campsite and try to steal your food or shiny objects. They have been known to take car keys, jewelry, and even sandwiches!

These clever birds eat almost anything. They eat insects, mice, eggs, fruit, and even small fish. Wekas use their strong beaks to turn over rocks and logs to find food underneath.

Weka families work together. The mother and father both help to build the nest and look after their babies. Young wekas stay with their parents for several months, learning how to find food and stay safe.

Wekas make many different sounds to communicate. They can grunt, bark, and make loud calling sounds, especially at dawn and dusk. If you hear a loud "weka-weka-weka" sound, you know there's a weka nearby!

Today, wekas only live in certain parts of New Zealand. They used to live everywhere, but now they are mainly found on the West Coast of the South Island and some islands around New Zealand.

### Comprehension Questions - Passage 3

**Literal Questions:**

1. How big is a weka?
2. What might a weka steal from campers?
3. When do wekas make the most noise?
4. Where do wekas mainly live now?

**Inferential Questions:** 5. Why might a weka's curiosity sometimes get it into trouble? 6. Why is it helpful that wekas eat "almost anything"? 7. What does it tell us that both weka parents help raise their babies?

**Critical Thinking:** 8. Compare a weka to a kiwi. How are they similar and different? 9. Why do you think wekas don't live everywhere in New Zealand anymore? 10. Would you like to meet a weka? Why or why not?

## Vocabulary Building Activities

### Bird Words Glossary

**Students create their own picture dictionary:**

- **Nocturnal:** Active at night (Draw a moon and stars)
- **Nectar:** Sweet liquid inside flowers (Draw a flower with a drop)
- **Pollen:** Yellow dust that helps plants make seeds (Draw a bee covered in yellow dots)
- **Investigate:** To look carefully and find out about something (Draw a magnifying glass)
- **Curious:** Wanting to know about everything (Draw a question mark)

### Word Families

**Students sort words from the passages:**

**Movement Words:** run, walk, swim, fly, hop
**Sound Words:** whistle, click, grunt, bark, call
**Describing Words:** tiny, strong, beautiful, intelligent, curious
**Body Parts:** wings, beak, feathers, tongue, legs

## Extension Activities

### Research Project Options

**Students choose one to investigate further:**

1. **Kiwi Conservation:** Find out about kiwi protection programs
2. **Māori Bird Stories:** Research traditional stories about tuis or other birds
3. **Bird Watching:** Create a guide to birds you can see around school
4. **Helping Native Birds:** Make a poster about how to help New Zealand birds

### Creative Responses

1. **Write a story** about meeting one of these birds
2. **Draw a comic strip** showing a curious weka's adventure
3. **Create a song** using tui sounds and calls
4. **Design a garden** that would attract native birds

### Cross-Curricular Connections

- **Art:** Draw or paint New Zealand native birds
- **Music:** Listen to recordings of bird calls and create music inspired by them
- **Technology:** Use apps to identify birds or record bird sounds
- **Mathematics:** Create graphs showing bird sizes or count birds around school

## Assessment Opportunities

### Formative Assessment

- **Reading conferences** with individual students
- **Comprehension question discussions** in small groups
- **Vocabulary games** and word sorting activities
- **Observation checklist** for reading strategies used

### Summative Assessment Task

**"New Zealand Bird Expert" Project**

Students create a fact file about a New Zealand native bird (not kiwi, tui, or weka):

**Requirements:**

1. **Physical description** with picture or drawing
2. **Habitat** - where the bird lives
3. **Diet** - what the bird eats
4. **Special features** - what makes this bird unique
5. **Conservation status** - is the bird endangered?
6. **Fun fact** - something interesting most people don't know

**Success Criteria:**

- Information is accurate and comes from reliable sources
- Uses descriptive vocabulary appropriately
- Includes at least one personal connection or reflection
- Presentation is clear and well-organized

## Teacher Notes

# Preparation Tips

- **Pre-read passages** to identify challenging vocabulary
- **Prepare audio recordings** for students who need listening support
- **Gather bird photos** or books for visual references
- **Contact local bird experts** who might visit the classroom

### Discussion Points

- **Connect to students' experiences** - have they seen these birds?
- **Address conservation sensitively** - focus on positive actions
- **Celebrate New Zealand's unique wildlife**
- **Link to local environmental initiatives**

### Assessment Focus

- **Comprehension strategies** students use independently
- **Vocabulary development** and correct usage
- **Personal connections** to strengthen engagement
- **Information literacy** skills for research tasks

This unit builds reading comprehension while fostering appreciation for New Zealand's unique wildlife and developing students' research and critical thinking skills.
