# Year 5 Physical Education: Traditional Māori Games and Movement
**New Zealand Curriculum Level 3**

## Learning Objectives
Students will be able to:
- Understand the cultural significance of traditional Māori games
- Develop movement skills through authentic cultural activities
- Appreciate the connection between physical activity, culture, and community
- Demonstrate respect for Māori cultural practices
- Work collaboratively and show good sportsmanship

---

## Cultural Considerations and Protocols

**Essential Requirements:**
- **Consult with local iwi or Māori cultural advisors** before beginning this unit
- **Invite Māori community members** to teach authentic games where possible
- **Approach with respect and cultural humility**
- **Acknowledge the origins** and significance of each game
- **Emphasize learning and respect** rather than competition alone
- **Use correct pronunciation** of Māori terms and practice together

**Cultural Context:**
Traditional Māori games were not just entertainment—they developed skills needed for daily life, strengthened communities, and passed on cultural knowledge. These games taught agility, strength, strategy, and cooperation essential for survival and community wellbeing.

---

## Unit Overview (8 lessons)

This unit introduces students to traditional Māori games while developing fundamental movement skills, cultural understanding, and community values through respectful participation.

## Lesson 1: Introduction to Māori Games and Cultural Context

### Cultural Introduction (15 minutes)
**"Games with Purpose and Meaning"**

**Opening Karakia or Acknowledgment:**
Begin with appropriate cultural acknowledgment and brief explanation of why we start this way.

**Historical Context:**
- Traditional Māori games were part of daily life
- Games developed skills needed for hunting, gathering, and protection
- Competitions strengthened relationships between whānau and iwi
- Many games taught patience, strategy, and respect for others
- Games were often played during hui (gatherings) and celebrations

**Modern Connection:**
- These games are still enjoyed today
- They help us understand Māori culture and values
- Physical skills developed transfer to modern sports and activities
- Playing together builds community just like it did traditionally

### Game Demonstration: Poi Rakau (15 minutes)
**"Stick Games for Rhythm and Coordination"**

**Cultural Significance:**
Poi rakau developed hand-eye coordination, rhythm, and concentration needed for traditional activities like using tools and weapons.

**Equipment:**
- 40cm wooden dowels or light sticks (one per student)
- Space for students to sit in circle

**Basic Instructions:**
1. **Sitting Position:** Students sit cross-legged in circle
2. **Basic Hold:** Hold stick lightly in right hand, about middle
3. **Basic Throw:** Gently toss stick to person on right
4. **Catching:** Catch stick coming from left with left hand
5. **Rhythm:** Start slowly, build up speed as group improves

**Safety Rules:**
- Gentle throws only
- Keep sticks low
- Focus on your neighbor receiving the stick
- Stop immediately if anyone calls "stop"

**Progression:**
- Start with just passing around circle
- Add rhythm/chanting when comfortable
- Try changing directions
- Advanced: Two sticks per person

### Reflection Circle (15 minutes)
**"What Did We Learn?"**

**Discussion Questions:**
- How did it feel to play a game that's hundreds of years old?
- What skills did poi rakau help us practice?
- How did working together help everyone succeed?
- What was challenging about this game?
- How might this game have helped Māori people in traditional times?

**Key Learning Points:**
- Traditional games developed real-life skills
- Community cooperation makes games more enjoyable
- Practice and patience help everyone improve
- Cultural activities connect us to history and each other

## Lesson 2: Ti Rakau - Stick Games for Agility

### Warm-up and Cultural Context (10 minutes)
**"Preparing Mind and Body"**

**Physical Warm-up:**
- Joint mobility: shoulders, wrists, ankles
- Light jogging or walking
- Coordination exercises: patting head and rubbing tummy

**Cultural Context for Ti Rakau:**
- Developed agility and quick reflexes
- Prepared warriors for combat situations
- Taught focus and concentration
- Practiced in groups to build teamwork

### Ti Rakau Instruction and Practice (25 minutes)
**"Quick Thinking and Movement"**

**Equipment:**
- 30cm light sticks (bamboo or dowel)
- Cones to mark boundaries
- Whistle or bell for signals

**Basic Ti Rakau Rules:**
1. **Setup:** Students stand in two lines facing each other, about 3 meters apart
2. **Starting Position:** Each student holds one stick vertically on the ground
3. **The Challenge:** When signal is given, release stick and run to catch neighbor's stick before it falls
4. **Success:** Catching any stick keeps you in the game
5. **Challenge:** Miss a stick and do 5 jumping jacks before rejoining

**Progression Levels:**
- **Level 1:** Walk to catch neighbor's stick
- **Level 2:** Jog to catch stick
- **Level 3:** Run to catch stick
- **Level 4:** Skip or hop to catch stick
- **Level 5:** Catch stick two places away

**Variations:**
- Change direction of movement
- Use different locomotor skills
- Add multiple sticks per person
- Create team challenges

### Cool Down and Reflection (10 minutes)
**"Thinking About Our Learning"**

**Physical Cool Down:**
- Slow walking
- Gentle stretching
- Deep breathing

**Reflection Questions:**
- What strategies helped you be successful?
- How did the game get easier with practice?
- What would happen if people didn't cooperate?
- How do you think this helped traditional Māori develop quick thinking?

## Lesson 3: Takaro Rōpū - Group Running Games

### Cultural Introduction (10 minutes)
**"Running Games for Strength and Strategy"**

**Traditional Context:**
- Developed endurance for long journeys
- Taught strategy and teamwork
- Built community spirit through group activities
- Practiced skills needed for hunting and gathering

### Oma Kapua (Cloud Running) (15 minutes)
**"Running Like Clouds Across the Sky"**

**Cultural Story:**
Clouds move across the sky in groups, sometimes fast, sometimes slow, always together. This game teaches us to move like clouds—smoothly and as a team.

**Game Setup:**
- Large playing area with clear boundaries
- Students in groups of 6-8
- Each group is a "cloud"

**Basic Rules:**
1. **Cloud Formation:** Groups link arms or hold hands
2. **Cloud Movement:** Move together across playing area
3. **Challenge:** Navigate around obstacles without breaking apart
4. **Wind Changes:** Teacher calls out different movement speeds
5. **Storm:** Groups must find shelter (designated areas) together

**Movement Variations:**
- Slow drift (walking)
- Gentle breeze (jogging)
- Strong wind (running)
- Storm (fast movement to shelter)

### Karetao (Traditional Tag) (15 minutes)
**"Strategic Pursuit Game"**

**Cultural Background:**
This game developed the hunting and evasion skills needed for survival, while teaching strategy and quick decision-making.

**Game Rules:**
1. **Setup:** One or two students are "hunters," others are "prey"
2. **Safe Zones:** Designated areas where "prey" can rest briefly
3. **Strategy Element:** "Prey" can help each other by creating distractions
4. **Role Changes:** When tagged, students become hunters
5. **Community Rule:** No one stays hunter for more than 2 minutes

**Skill Development:**
- Agility and change of direction
- Spatial awareness
- Strategic thinking
- Cooperation and helping others

### Reflection and Cool Down (5 minutes)
**"Learning from Movement"**

- How did working as a team make the games more fun?
- What strategies worked best for avoiding being caught?
- How do these games help us think quickly and move well?

## Lesson 4: Kī-o-Rahi - Traditional Ball Game

### Cultural and Historical Context (10 minutes)
**"The Ancient Game of Strategy"**

**Kī-o-Rahi Background:**
- Over 800 years old
- Played by both men and women
- Combines elements of netball, rugby, and touch
- Develops strategy, teamwork, and ball skills
- Still played competitively today throughout New Zealand

**Game Values:**
- Whakatōhea (cooperation)
- Manaakitanga (caring for others)
- Rangatiratanga (leadership)

### Modified Kī-o-Rahi for Year 5 (30 minutes)
**"Learning the Sacred Game"**

**Equipment:**
- Soft balls (size 3 or 4)
- Cones to mark circular playing area
- Colored bibs for teams

**Simplified Field Setup:**
- Large circle (20m diameter if possible)
- Central circle (3m diameter)
- Four markers around outside edge

**Basic Game Rules:**
1. **Teams:** Two teams of 6-8 players
2. **Objective:** Score points by getting ball to designated targets
3. **Movement:** Pass ball using hands, no running with ball
4. **Defense:** Gentle tagging only, no tackling
5. **Rotation:** All players get chances in different positions

**Scoring Methods:**
- **Kī:** Touch ball to central target (2 points)
- **Rahi:** Pass ball around outside circle (1 point)
- **Teamwork Bonus:** All team members touch ball before scoring (extra point)

**Modified Rules for Safety and Inclusion:**
- No contact except gentle tagging
- All players must touch ball before team can score
- Compulsory rotation of positions every 5 minutes
- "Freeze" rule - game stops when teacher calls it

### Game Reflection (5 minutes)
**"Understanding the Deeper Game"**

- What strategies worked best for your team?
- How did everyone contribute to the game?
- What was challenging about working together?
- Why do you think this game has been played for 800 years?

## Lesson 5: Mū Tōrere - Strategy and Mind Games

### Introduction to Mental and Physical Games (10 minutes)
**"Games for Mind and Body"**

**Cultural Context:**
Not all traditional games were purely physical. Mū tōrere and similar games developed strategic thinking, patience, and problem-solving—essential skills for leadership and decision-making.

**Connection to Physical Education:**
- Mental skills support physical performance
- Strategy games develop focus and concentration
- Patience and persistence transfer to physical challenges
- Social skills from games apply to team sports

### Mū Tōrere Board Game (20 minutes)
**"The Strategy of the Eight Points"**

**Equipment:**
- Mū tōrere boards (can be drawn on paper or made with stones)
- Counters or tokens for each player
- Multiple sets so students can play in pairs

**Game Rules:**
1. **Setup:** Star-shaped board with 8 points and center
2. **Players:** Two players with 4 counters each
3. **Objective:** Block opponent so they cannot move
4. **Movement:** Counters move to adjacent empty points
5. **Center Rule:** Can only move to center from point next to opponent's counter

**Learning Progression:**
- Teacher demonstrates with large board
- Students practice with partners
- Mini-tournament with rotation
- Discussion of strategies discovered

### Physical Strategy Game: Whakawātea (15 minutes)
**"Moving Strategy"**

**Setup:**
- Large grid marked on ground (3x3 or 4x4)
- Two teams with different colored markers
- Students are the "pieces" on the board

**Rules:**
1. **Teams:** Take turns placing one team member on grid
2. **Objective:** Get three in a row (like tic-tac-toe)
3. **Movement Phase:** After all pieces placed, can move to adjacent squares
4. **Physical Challenge:** Must perform exercise to move (jumping jacks, balance poses)
5. **Strategy:** Plan moves ahead while staying physically active

### Reflection (5 minutes)
**"Mind and Body Connection"**

- How did thinking strategically affect your physical performance?
- What skills from strategy games might help in sports?
- How did patience and planning help you succeed?

## Lesson 6: Whai - String Games for Dexterity

### Cultural Introduction to String Games (10 minutes)
**"Stories in String"**

**Traditional Significance:**
- Developed fine motor skills and dexterity
- Told stories and preserved cultural knowledge
- Practiced during quiet times and long winter evenings
- Passed down through generations as cultural teaching

**Connection to Physical Education:**
- Fine motor skills support gross motor development
- Hand-eye coordination transfers to ball skills
- Concentration and focus improve overall performance
- Cultural learning enhances social and emotional wellbeing

### Basic Whai (String Figures) (25 minutes)
**"Creating Pictures with String"**

**Equipment:**
- Loops of string (about 1.5m circumference)
- One loop per student
- Instruction cards with basic figures

**Basic String Figures to Learn:**
1. **Te Whare (The House)**
   - Simple rectangular figure
   - Represents shelter and home

2. **Te Waka (The Canoe)**
   - Boat-like shape
   - Represents travel and journey

3. **Ngā Whetu (The Stars)**
   - Multiple points representing stars
   - Connects to navigation and guidance

4. **Te Rā (The Sun)**
   - Circular pattern
   - Represents life and energy

**Learning Process:**
- Teacher demonstrates slowly
- Students work in pairs to help each other
- Focus on patience and persistence
- Celebrate different learning speeds

**Cultural Storytelling:**
With each figure, share brief story or cultural significance.

### Partner Teaching Activity (10 minutes)
**"Sharing Our Knowledge"**

Students who master a figure teach it to others:
- Develops leadership skills
- Reinforces learning through teaching
- Builds community and cooperation
- Honors traditional learning methods

## Lesson 7: Tapu Ae - Traditional Athletic Challenges

### Warm-up and Cultural Context (10 minutes)
**"Preparing for Athletic Challenges"**

**Traditional Athletic Development:**
- Built strength and endurance for daily tasks
- Prepared individuals for community responsibilities
- Developed personal discipline and self-improvement
- Created friendly competition that strengthened relationships

**Physical Warm-up:**
- Dynamic stretching
- Light jogging
- Joint mobility exercises
- Practice movements that will be used in challenges

### Traditional Athletic Stations (30 minutes)
**"Testing Our Abilities"**

**Station Setup:** Four stations with 6-7 minutes each, plus transition time

**Station 1: Kopapa (Leaping)**
- **Skill:** Standing long jump and running long jump
- **Traditional Use:** Crossing streams, navigating terrain
- **Activity:** Measure and record personal bests, focus on improvement
- **Cultural Connection:** Leaping ability was essential for travel

**Station 2: Whakawhetai (Balance Challenges)**
- **Skill:** Balance on different surfaces and objects
- **Traditional Use:** Walking on logs, navigating rocky areas
- **Activity:** Balance beam walking, one-foot balance challenges
- **Cultural Connection:** Sure-footedness kept people safe

**Station 3: Ringa Kaha (Strength Challenges)**
- **Skill:** Functional strength movements
- **Traditional Use:** Lifting, carrying, building
- **Activity:** Partner resistance exercises, bear crawls
- **Cultural Connection:** Strength helped with daily work

**Station 4: Tarawera (Throwing Accuracy)**
- **Skill:** Accurate throwing at targets
- **Traditional Use:** Hunting, fishing, tool use
- **Activity:** Underarm throwing at various targets
- **Cultural Connection:** Accuracy provided food and protection

### Personal Challenge Setting (5 minutes)
**"Setting Goals Like Our Ancestors"**

Students identify one area for personal improvement and set a goal for continued practice.

## Lesson 8: Tournament Day and Cultural Celebration

### Opening Ceremony (10 minutes)
**"Honoring the Games and Each Other"**

**Cultural Acknowledgment:**
- Acknowledge the origins of the games
- Thank any community members who have shared knowledge
- Set intentions for respectful competition
- Remind everyone of cultural values: respect, cooperation, enjoyment

### Multi-Game Tournament (30 minutes)
**"Celebrating Our Learning"**

**Tournament Format:**
- Mixed teams for inclusive participation
- Multiple games happening simultaneously
- Emphasis on participation and sportsmanship
- Rotating so everyone tries different activities

**Games Included:**
- Poi rakau demonstrations
- Ti rakau challenges
- Modified kī-o-rahi matches
- Whai (string games) sharing
- Athletic challenges from previous lesson

**Scoring System:**
- Points for participation and effort
- Bonus points for helping others
- Recognition for improvement shown
- Team spirit and cultural respect valued

### Closing Circle and Reflection (5 minutes)
**"Honoring Our Learning Journey"**

**Celebration Elements:**
- Acknowledge everyone's participation
- Share highlights from the tournament
- Reflect on cultural learning gained
- Plan how to continue using these games
- Close with appropriate cultural acknowledgment

**Reflection Questions:**
- What did you learn about Māori culture through these games?
- Which games would you like to continue playing?
- How did playing traditional games feel different from modern sports?
- What values from these games can you use in other parts of life?

## Assessment

### Formative Assessment Throughout Unit
- **Daily observation** of skill development and cultural understanding
- **Peer feedback** during partner and group activities
- **Self-reflection** questions at end of each lesson
- **Participation quality** rather than just participation quantity

### Summative Assessment: Cultural Learning Portfolio

**Portfolio Components:**

**1. Movement Skill Development (30%)**
- Improvement in coordination, balance, and agility
- Ability to follow game rules and safety procedures
- Demonstration of fundamental movement skills

**2. Cultural Understanding and Respect (40%)**
- Respectful participation in cultural activities
- Understanding of games' traditional significance
- Appropriate use of Māori terms and concepts
- Demonstration of cultural values during activities

**3. Social and Cooperative Skills (30%)**
- Effective collaboration during team activities
- Supportive behavior toward all classmates
- Leadership shown in teaching others
- Positive sportsmanship throughout unit

### Assessment Methods
- **Observation checklists** for specific skills and behaviors
- **Student self-assessment** using age-appropriate reflection tools
- **Peer feedback** on cooperation and cultural respect
- **Portfolio evidence** including photos, reflections, and learning artifacts

## Differentiation Strategies

### For Students with Higher Physical Abilities
- **Leadership roles** in teaching and demonstrating
- **Advanced variations** of games with increased complexity
- **Cultural research** projects about traditional Māori sports
- **Mentoring opportunities** supporting classmates

### For Students Needing Physical Support
- **Modified equipment** (lighter balls, shorter distances)
- **Alternative roles** (scorer, timekeeper, encourager)
- **Adapted movements** that maintain cultural integrity
- **Focus on personal improvement** rather than comparison

### For Students Learning English
- **Visual demonstrations** rather than complex verbal instructions
- **Buddy system** with bilingual peers when possible
- **Cultural connections** to students' own traditional games
- **Key vocabulary** support with visual cards

### For Students with Cultural Connections to Māori Heritage
- **Knowledge sharing** opportunities when appropriate
- **Family involvement** in cultural accuracy checking
- **Extended learning** about whānau or iwi specific traditions
- **Leadership roles** in cultural protocol when suitable

This unit honors traditional Māori culture while developing physical literacy, social skills, and cultural understanding. Students gain appreciation for the wisdom embedded in traditional games while building fitness, coordination, and community connections that will benefit them throughout their lives.
