# Year 9 English: Poetry Unit - Identity and Belonging in Aotearoa

**New Zealand Curriculum Level 5**

## Learning Objectives

Students will be able to:

- Analyze how poets explore themes of identity and belonging
- Understand and use poetic devices effectively
- Create original poetry expressing personal and cultural identity
- Connect poetry to their own experiences and New Zealand contexts

---

## Unit Overview (8 lessons)

### Lesson 1: What is Identity? Introduction to the Unit

#### Hook Activity (15 minutes)

**"Identity Graffiti Wall"**

Students move around the room writing words that describe their identity on large sheets of paper:

- Where I'm from...
- I am someone who...
- My family/whānau...
- I belong to...
- I feel proud when...
- I feel different when...

Create a class "graffiti wall" of identity words and phrases.

#### Poem Introduction (20 minutes)

**"Where I'm From" Template Poem**

Read George Ella Lyon's "Where I'm From" poem, then students create their own version:

```
I am from...
[specific ordinary items from your childhood]

I am from...
[places and environments that shaped you]

I am from...
[family traditions, sayings, foods]

I am from...
[names of relatives, family stories]

I am from...
[experiences that made you who you are]
```

**New Zealand Example Starter:**

```
I am from gumboots by the back door,
from hokey pokey ice cream and fish and chips wrapped in newspaper.

I am from the sound of tui in the morning,
pohutukawa blooming red at Christmas.

I am from "sweet as" and "choice bro,"
from hangi and kai shared with many hands.

I am from stories of great-grandmother who sailed from Scotland,
and grandfather who fought at Gallipoli...
```

#### Sharing and Reflection (10 minutes)

Students share one line from their poem and explain why it's important to their identity.

### Lesson 2: Māori Identity in Poetry

#### Cultural Context (15 minutes)

**Whakapapa and Identity**

Discuss the concept of whakapapa (genealogy/connections) in Māori culture:

- How identity connects to ancestors, land, and iwi
- The importance of knowing where you come from
- How colonization has affected Māori identity

#### Poem Study: Hone Tuwhare - "Rain" (20 minutes)

Read and analyze Hone Tuwhare's poem "Rain":

```
I can hear you
making small holes
in the silence
rain

If I were deaf
in a glade of pohutukawa trees
I would still know you
by your smell

When the earth is hard
you strike it gently...
```

**Analysis Questions:**

1. How does Tuwhare personify rain?
2. What New Zealand natural elements does he reference?
3. How might this poem reflect a Māori worldview of connection to nature?
4. What poetic devices does he use? (personification, imagery, repetition)

#### Creative Response (10 minutes)

Students write a short poem addressing a natural element from their own environment, using personification as Tuwhare does.

### Lesson 3: Migrant Identity and Finding Home

#### Discussion Starter (10 minutes)

**"Home is..." Brainstorm**

Students complete: "Home is..." in as many ways as possible:

- A place, a feeling, people, memories, food, language, etc.

#### Poem Study: Apirana Taylor - "Sad Joke on a Marae" (25 minutes)

Read Apirana Taylor's poem exploring mixed heritage identity:

Analyze themes of:

- Belonging to multiple cultures
- Feeling "in between" identities
- Challenges of mixed heritage in New Zealand
- The marae as a place of identity and belonging

**Discussion Questions:**

1. What internal conflict does the speaker experience?
2. How does the setting (marae) add to the poem's meaning?
3. What does this poem suggest about identity in multicultural New Zealand?
4. How does the title "Sad Joke" reflect the speaker's feelings?

#### Personal Reflection (10 minutes)

**Identity Complexity Web**

Students create a visual web showing the different aspects of their identity and any tensions or connections between them.

### Lesson 4: Contemporary Voices - Slam Poetry and Spoken Word

#### Introduction to Slam Poetry (10 minutes)

**What is Slam Poetry?**

- Performance poetry focused on personal experience
- Often addresses social issues, identity, injustice
- Emphasizes rhythm, emotion, and audience connection

#### Video Analysis (15 minutes)

**Watch New Zealand Slam Poets**

View performances by contemporary NZ poets like:

- Dominic Hoey (Tourettes)
- Tusiata Avia
- Mohamed Hassan

**Analysis Focus:**

- How do they use rhythm and repetition?
- What personal experiences do they share?
- How do they connect personal identity to broader social issues?
- What performance techniques make their poetry powerful?

#### Slam Poetry Elements (15 minutes)

**Techniques for Powerful Performance Poetry:**

1. **Personal storytelling** - specific, vivid details
2. **Repetition and rhythm** - creates emphasis and flow
3. **Metaphor and simile** - makes abstract concepts concrete
4. **Lists and catalogs** - builds intensity
5. **Direct address** - speaks to audience or specific person
6. **Vulnerability** - honest emotion creates connection

#### Planning Your Own (5 minutes)

Students brainstorm a personal experience or aspect of identity they could explore in slam style.

### Lesson 5: Writing Workshop - Identity Poetry

#### Warm-up (5 minutes)

**Word Association Identity Chain**
Students write their name vertically and create an identity word for each letter.

#### Poetry Forms Review (10 minutes)

**Choosing Your Style:**

**Free Verse:** No regular pattern, natural speech rhythms
**Cinquain:** 5-line poem with specific structure
**Slam/Spoken Word:** Written for performance
**Haiku:** 3 lines capturing a moment
**Narrative Poem:** Tells a story

#### Individual Writing Time (30 minutes)

**Identity Poetry Options:**

Students choose one to develop:

1. **"Where I'm From" Extended Version**

   - Develop their earlier poem with more specific details
   - Include family stories, cultural traditions, places

2. **"Two Cultures" Poem**

   - Explore belonging to multiple cultures or communities
   - Show tensions or celebrations of mixed identity

3. **"Finding My Voice" Narrative**

   - Tell the story of a moment they discovered something about themselves
   - Include dialogue, setting, character development

4. **"What They Don't Know About Me" List Poem**

   - Reveal hidden aspects of identity
   - Challenge stereotypes or assumptions

5. **"Home" Sensory Poem**
   - Use all five senses to describe what home means
   - Could be a place, person, or feeling

**Writing Support:**

- Sentence starters and prompts available
- Individual conferences with teacher
- Peer brainstorming partnerships

#### Sharing Circle (5 minutes)

Volunteers share one powerful line from their work-in-progress.

---

### Lesson 6: Peer Review and Revision Workshop

#### Peer Review Process (25 minutes)

**Poetry Partners Protocol:**

**Step 1: Read Aloud (5 minutes per person)**

- Poet reads their work aloud to partner
- Listener just listens, no interrupting

**Step 2: Response (5 minutes per person)**

- What images stuck with you?
- What emotions did you feel?
- What do you want to know more about?

**Step 3: Questions (5 minutes per person)**

- What is the poet trying to express?
- Where could they add more specific details?
- Which lines are strongest? Why?

**Step 4: Suggestions (5 minutes per person)**

- One specific suggestion for improvement
- One area where they could expand or develop further

#### Revision Time (20 minutes)

Students revise their poems based on feedback:

- Add specific details and imagery
- Strengthen word choices
- Improve line breaks and rhythm
- Consider performance elements if applicable

#### Final Draft Preparation (5 minutes)

Students prepare clean final drafts for assessment and possible performance.

---

### Lesson 7: Poetry Performance Café

#### Set-up (10 minutes)

Transform classroom into poetry café:

- Arrange desks in small groups
- Dim lights, play soft background music
- Optional: provide hot drinks or snacks

#### Performance Guidelines (5 minutes)

**Poetry Café Etiquette:**

- Respect for all voices and experiences
- Supportive audience behavior
- Optional participation (no pressure to share)
- Constructive feedback only

#### Poetry Performances (30 minutes)

**Café Format:**

- Students take turns performing at their tables (5 minutes per table)
- Rotate so everyone hears different poems
- Teacher circulates to listen and support

**Performance Options:**

- Read aloud with expression
- Memorize and perform
- Use props or visual aids
- Collaborate with others

#### Celebration and Reflection (5 minutes)

**Appreciation Circle:**

- What poems moved you most?
- What did you learn about your classmates?
- How did performing feel different from just writing?

---

### Lesson 8: Assessment and Extension

#### Portfolio Reflection (20 minutes)

**Poetry Portfolio Assembly:**

Students compile:

1. **Final identity poem** with revision notes
2. **Analysis of one studied poem** (chosen from unit)
3. **Reflection essay** (300 words): "How has studying poetry changed my understanding of identity and belonging?"

**Reflection Questions:**

- What did you discover about your own identity through writing?
- How do the poets we studied express their sense of belonging?
- What role does poetry play in exploring personal and cultural identity?
- How has your appreciation for poetry changed?

#### Extension Activities (25 minutes)

**Choose Your Own Adventure:**

**Option 1: Community Poetry Project**

- Research local poets or poetry events
- Plan a class contribution to school literary magazine
- Organize poetry reading for other classes

**Option 2: Historical Poetry Investigation**

- Research how poetry has been used during significant NZ historical events
- Find poems about the World Wars, women's suffrage, land rights
- Create a timeline of NZ poetry and historical context

**Option 3: Poetry and Social Justice**

- Research how poets address social issues
- Write response poems to current issues affecting young people
- Plan a "poetry for change" presentation

**Option 4: Multicultural Poetry Anthology**

- Collect poems from poets representing different cultures in NZ
- Create themed anthology with introduction and analysis
- Include original poems inspired by different cultural traditions

#### Unit Wrap-up (5 minutes)

**Exit Ticket:**
"One line from any poem in this unit that will stay with me is..."

## Assessment

### Formative Assessment

- **Daily check-ins** during writing workshops
- **Peer feedback** quality and constructiveness
- **Class discussions** - depth of engagement with themes
- **Portfolio drafts** - evidence of revision and growth

### Summative Assessment: Poetry Portfolio

**Portfolio Components (Total: 100 marks)**

1. **Original Identity Poem (40 marks)**

   - Clear expression of personal/cultural identity (10 marks)
   - Effective use of poetic devices (10 marks)
   - Specific, vivid imagery and details (10 marks)
   - Overall impact and creativity (10 marks)

2. **Poetry Analysis Essay (30 marks)**

   - Understanding of chosen poem's themes (10 marks)
   - Analysis of poetic techniques (10 marks)
   - Personal connections and insights (10 marks)

3. **Reflection Essay (20 marks)**

   - Thoughtful consideration of learning journey (10 marks)
   - Clear writing and organization (10 marks)

4. **Process Documentation (10 marks)**
   - Evidence of drafting and revision (5 marks)
   - Quality of peer feedback given and received (5 marks)

### Performance Assessment (Optional)

- **Presentation skills** - voice, expression, engagement
- **Interpretation** - understanding of poem's meaning through performance
- **Audience awareness** - connection with listeners
