# Year 2 Visual Arts: Patterns in Nature - Māori Design Elements

**New Zealand Curriculum Level 1-2**

## Learning Objectives

Students will be able to:

- Observe and identify patterns in the natural New Zealand environment
- Understand and respectfully use basic Māori design elements (kōwhaiwhai)
- Create original artworks inspired by nature patterns
- Use a variety of art materials and techniques
- Appreciate the connection between nature, culture, and art

---

## Cultural Considerations and Protocols

**Important:** This unit requires respectful approach to Māori cultural elements. Please:

- **Consult with local iwi or Māori cultural advisors** before beginning
- **Use traditional designs respectfully** - focus on learning patterns, not copying sacred elements
- **Acknowledge the source** of all Māori design elements used
- **Emphasize connection to nature** rather than attempting to replicate traditional taonga
- **Invite Māori community members** to share knowledge where appropriate

---

## Unit Overview (8 lessons)

This unit explores how Māori artists traditionally observed nature to create beautiful patterns, encouraging Year 2 students to make similar observations and create their own nature-inspired artworks using appropriate Māori design principles.

---

## Lesson 1: Exploring Patterns in Our Natural World

### Nature Walk and Observation (20 minutes)

**"Pattern Detectives in Nature"**

Take students outside to observe patterns in school grounds:

**Pattern Hunt Checklist:**

- [ ] Leaf shapes and vein patterns
- [ ] Tree bark textures
- [ ] Flower petal arrangements
- [ ] Rock and stone patterns
- [ ] Bird feather designs
- [ ] Insect wing patterns
- [ ] Cloud formations
- [ ] Water ripples (if available)

**Observation Tools:**

- Magnifying glasses for close-up viewing
- Digital cameras or tablets for recording
- Clipboards with observation sheets
- Colored pencils for quick sketches

### Pattern Recording Activity (15 minutes)

**"Capturing What We See"**

Students choose 3 different natural patterns to record:

- Simple line drawings showing pattern structure
- Written descriptions using pattern vocabulary
- Color notes about what they observe

**Pattern Vocabulary Introduction:**

- **Repeating:** Same shape over and over
- **Spirals:** Round patterns that curl
- **Lines:** Straight or wavy marks
- **Shapes:** Circles, triangles, curves
- **Symmetry:** Same on both sides

### Sharing Circle (10 minutes)

**"What Amazing Patterns Did We Find?"**

Students share one pattern they found most interesting:

- Show their drawing to the class
- Explain where they found it
- Describe what makes it special
- Discuss how it might be useful to the plant or animal

## Lesson 2: Introduction to Māori Design - Respectful Learning

### Cultural Introduction (15 minutes)

**"Learning About Māori Artists and Nature"**

**Presentation Points:**

- Māori artists have always been inspired by the natural world of Aotearoa
- Traditional patterns (kōwhaiwhai) often come from observing nature
- These patterns have special meanings and connections to the environment
- We can learn from this approach while creating our own original designs

**Show Examples of Traditional Patterns:**

- **Koru:** Unfurling fern frond representing new life and growth
- **Kowhai:** Simple curved lines inspired by kowhai flower
- **Pikorua:** Twisted cord pattern representing life's journey
- **Poutama:** Step pattern representing learning and achievement

**Important Message:** "We're learning how Māori artists think about nature, so we can create our own special patterns inspired by what we see around us."

### Nature Connection Activity (20 minutes)

**"Finding the Koru in Our World"**

Focus on the koru (spiral) as most accessible pattern for Year 2:

**Koru Hunt:**

- Look for spiral patterns in nature photos and real objects
- Fern fronds (fiddle heads)
- Snail shells
- Flower buds opening
- Ocean waves curling
- Sheep horns
- Vine tendrils

**Koru Drawing Practice:**

- Start with finger tracing in air
- Practice on mini whiteboards
- Use large arm movements for flow
- Focus on smooth, continuous curves

### Reflection Discussion (10 minutes)

**"Why Do You Think Māori Artists Loved the Koru?"**

Guide students to understand:

- Spirals represent growth and new beginnings
- Nature is full of these beautiful patterns
- Artists can learn by watching carefully
- Patterns can have meaning and tell stories

## Lesson 3: Observational Drawing - New Zealand Native Plants

### Introduction to Native Plants (10 minutes)

**"Special Plants of Aotearoa"**

Show images and real specimens (if available):

- **Ponga (Tree fern):** Large spiraling fronds
- **Kowhai:** Delicate compound leaves and bright flowers
- **Pohutukawa:** Feathery red flowers and tough leaves
- **Rata:** Dramatic red blooms
- **Manuka:** Small white flowers in clusters
- **Harakeke (Flax):** Long sword-like leaves with patterns

### Guided Drawing Session (25 minutes)

**"Drawing Like Nature Scientists and Artists"**

**Step-by-Step Process:**

1. **Look Carefully (5 minutes):** Observe chosen plant closely
2. **Basic Shapes First (5 minutes):** Start with simple shapes
3. **Add Details (10 minutes):** Include patterns, textures, lines
4. **Check and Improve (5 minutes):** Compare drawing to real plant

**Drawing Tips for Year 2:**

- Use light pencil lines first
- Look more than you draw
- It doesn't have to be perfect
- Focus on the patterns you see
- Add colors that match what you observe

**Materials:**

- A4 drawing paper
- 2B pencils for easy erasing
- Colored pencils or pastels
- Real plant specimens or high-quality photos

### Gallery Walk and Sharing (10 minutes)

**"Celebrating Our Observations"**

Students display their drawings and walk around to see others' work:

- Notice different ways of seeing the same plant
- Identify patterns others captured well
- Share positive observations about classmates' work
- Discuss which patterns were hardest/easiest to draw

## Lesson 4: Creating Patterns - Print Making with Natural Objects

### Print-Making Introduction (10 minutes)

**"Making Patterns with Real Nature"**

Demonstrate how to create prints using natural objects:

- Leaves with interesting shapes
- Flowers with clear structures
- Bark with interesting textures
- Seed pods with pattern potential

**Safety and Care:**

- Use objects that are already fallen (don't pick living plants)
- Handle natural materials gently
- Clean materials before and after use

### Printing Workshop (30 minutes)

**"Nature Print Patterns"**

**Materials for Each Student:**

- Selection of natural objects (leaves, flowers, textured bark pieces)
- Water-based printing inks or thick tempera paint
- Foam brushes or soft rollers
- Paper for printing
- Aprons or old shirts

**Process:**

1. **Choose Object:** Select natural item with interesting pattern
2. **Apply Ink:** Brush thin layer of ink onto object
3. **Position Paper:** Place paper carefully over inked object
4. **Pressure:** Rub gently but firmly to transfer pattern
5. **Lift Carefully:** Reveal the print
6. **Repeat:** Create multiple prints to make patterns

**Pattern Creation:**

- Repeat same print multiple times
- Rotate objects for different effects
- Overlap prints for complex patterns
- Use different colors for variety

### Reflection and Planning (5 minutes)

**"Which Prints Work Best for Patterns?"**

Discuss:

- Which natural objects made the clearest prints?
- How could we arrange these to make repeating patterns?
- What colors work well together?
- How do our prints compare to the traditional Māori patterns we learned about?

## Lesson 5: Kōwhaiwhai-Inspired Border Patterns

### Pattern Planning (15 minutes)

**"Designing Our Own Respectful Patterns"**

**Important Reminder:** We're creating our own patterns inspired by nature, not copying traditional designs.

**Planning Process:**

1. **Choose Natural Inspiration:** Select from nature observations (koru/spirals, leaf shapes, flower forms)
2. **Simplify the Shape:** Make it easy to repeat
3. **Plan Repetition:** How will pattern repeat along a line?
4. **Choose Colors:** What colors reflect the natural inspiration?

**Pattern Planning Sheet:**

- Draw basic shape 3 times in a row
- Try different arrangements (alternating, overlapping)
- Test color combinations
- Consider size and spacing

### Border Creation Workshop (25 minutes)

**"Painting Our Nature-Inspired Borders"**

**Materials:**

- Long strips of paper (5cm x 30cm)
- Acrylic or tempera paints
- Fine and medium brushes
- Water containers and paper towels
- Pencils for light guidelines

**Technique:**

1. **Light Pencil Guidelines:** Mark spacing for pattern repeats
2. **Start Simple:** Begin with basic shape in one color
3. **Add Details:** Include lines, dots, or additional colors
4. **Keep Consistent:** Try to make each repeat similar
5. **Flow and Rhythm:** Let the pattern have natural movement

**Color Suggestions:**

- Earth tones: browns, ochres, deep reds
- Plant colors: greens, yellows
- Natural combinations that reflect the inspiration source

### Quality Review (5 minutes)

**"Making Our Patterns Even Better"**

Students self-assess their work:

- Does the pattern repeat clearly?
- Are the colors appropriate for the natural inspiration?
- What would they do differently next time?
- How does it feel to create patterns like traditional artists?

## Lesson 6: Large-Scale Collaborative Artwork

### Project Introduction (10 minutes)

**"Creating a Class Nature Pattern Artwork"**

Explain collaborative project:

- Large paper divided into sections for each student
- Each student contributes one section using their developed patterns
- Final artwork represents collective learning about nature patterns
- Will be displayed to celebrate our cultural learning journey

### Individual Section Planning (15 minutes)

**"Your Contribution to Our Class Artwork"**

Each student receives a paper section (A5 size) and plans their contribution:

- Choose best pattern from previous lessons
- Adapt it for their section size
- Consider how it will connect with neighboring sections
- Plan color scheme that works with others

**Collaboration Discussion:**

- How can our individual patterns work together?
- What colors will help the whole artwork look unified?
- Should sections connect to each other or be independent?

### Artwork Creation (20 minutes)

**"Bringing Our Vision to Life"**

Students work on individual sections:

- Use techniques learned in previous lessons
- Focus on careful, quality work that represents their best effort
- Consider how their work contributes to the whole
- Ask for help and feedback from classmates

**Materials:**

- Pre-cut paper sections
- Range of brushes and art materials
- Reference materials from previous lessons
- Access to nature specimens for continued inspiration

---

## Lesson 7: Assembly and Finishing Touches

### Artwork Assembly (20 minutes)

**"Putting Our Masterpiece Together"**

Collaborative assembly process:

- Arrange individual sections to create cohesive whole
- Discuss placement and connections
- Make adjustments for visual flow
- Secure sections to backing paper or board

**Discussion During Assembly:**

- How do different patterns work together?
- What creates visual harmony in the overall piece?
- How does our artwork reflect our learning about nature patterns?
- What would traditional Māori artists think about our approach?

### Adding Connecting Elements (15 minutes)

**"Making It Flow Together"**

Add elements that unify the overall artwork:

- Border around the entire piece
- Connecting lines or shapes between sections
- Background wash or texture
- Title and artist credits

### Documentation and Reflection (10 minutes)

**"Recording Our Learning Journey"**

Create documentation:

- Photograph the completed artwork
- Individual written reflections on the process
- Group discussion about what was learned
- Planning for how to share with wider school community

**Reflection Questions:**

- What did you learn about patterns in nature?
- How did learning about Māori design help your own art?
- What was it like working collaboratively on artwork?
- What would you like to learn more about?

## Assessment

### Formative Assessment Throughout Unit

- **Daily observation** of student engagement and skill development
- **Process portfolio** including sketches, practice work, and reflections
- **Peer feedback** during collaborative activities
- **Self-reflection** questions at end of each lesson

### Summative Assessment Portfolio

**Portfolio Components:**

1. **Nature Observation Drawings (25%)**
   - Accuracy of pattern observation
   - Use of appropriate art techniques
   - Evidence of careful looking and recording

2. **Pattern Creation Work (35%)**
   - Original pattern designs inspired by nature
   - Technical skill with chosen art materials
   - Understanding of repetition and rhythm in patterns

3. **Collaborative Artwork Contribution (25%)**
   - Quality of individual section
   - Ability to work constructively with others
   - Consideration of how individual work contributes to whole

4. **Cultural Learning Reflection (15%)**
   - Understanding of respectful approach to cultural learning
   - Recognition of connection between nature and traditional Māori design
   - Appreciation for different ways of seeing and representing nature

### Assessment Criteria

**Developing (1-2):** Beginning to observe patterns and use art materials with support
**Proficient (3-4):** Independently observes patterns and creates artwork with appropriate techniques
**Advanced (5-6):** Demonstrates keen observation, skillful technique, and thoughtful cultural understanding

## Differentiation Strategies

### For Advanced Students

- **Extended observation challenges:** More complex pattern analysis
- **Leadership roles:** Help peers with technique and materials
- **Additional cultural research:** Learn about other indigenous art traditions
- **Technique exploration:** Try more complex art materials and methods

### for Students Needing Support

- **Simplified pattern focus:** Start with one basic pattern and develop thoroughly
- **Peer partnerships:** Work alongside stronger artists for encouragement
- **Modified materials:** Use easier-to-handle art tools and materials
- **Visual supports:** More demonstration and step-by-step visual guides

### For English Language Learners

- **Visual vocabulary cards:** Art and pattern terms with pictures
- **Multilingual pattern names:** Include pattern words in students' first languages
- **Cultural sharing:** Encourage sharing of pattern traditions from diverse backgrounds
- **Gesture and demonstration:** Rely on visual and kinesthetic learning

---

## Cultural Responsiveness and Safety

### Working with Māori Cultural Elements

- **Consultation protocol:** Always check with local iwi before using cultural elements
- **Appropriate attribution:** Acknowledge source and significance of traditional patterns
- **Educational focus:** Emphasize learning about approaches to nature, not copying sacred designs
- **Invitation to community:** Welcome Māori artists and cultural advisors to share knowledge

### Inclusive Practice

- **Diverse nature examples:** Include plants and patterns from various cultural backgrounds
- **Multiple art traditions:** Reference how different cultures find inspiration in nature
- **Accessible materials:** Ensure all students can participate regardless of fine motor skills
- **Celebratory approach:** Honor all students' cultural backgrounds and artistic expressions

- **Art club participation:** Continue developing artistic skills and interests
- **Seasonal observations:** Continue pattern documentation throughout the year
- **Advanced techniques:** Explore printmaking, weaving, or carving (age-appropriate)

- **Family art projects:** Create patterns inspired by family cultural traditions
- **Nature walks:** Continue pattern observation with families
- **Cultural sharing:** Have families share their own cultural art traditions
- **Home display:** Share completed artworks with families and community

This unit develops artistic skills, cultural awareness, and environmental observation while fostering respect for Māori approaches to art and nature. Students learn that art can be a bridge between cultures and a way to celebrate the natural world around us.
