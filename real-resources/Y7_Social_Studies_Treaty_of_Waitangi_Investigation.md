# Year 7 Social Studies: Treaty of Waitangi Investigation

**New Zealand Curriculum Level 4**

## Learning Objectives

Students will be able to:

- Understand the historical context and significance of the Treaty of Waitangi
- Analyze different perspectives on the Treaty from Māori and British viewpoints
- Examine the ongoing impact of the Treaty on modern New Zealand
- Develop research and critical thinking skills about historical sources

---

## Unit Overview (6 lessons)

### Lesson 1: Setting the Scene - New Zealand Before 1840

#### Hook Activity (15 minutes)

**Time Traveler's Dilemma**
"You've been sent back to 1839. You arrive in the Bay of Islands and meet both Māori rangatira and British missionaries. Both groups want different things. What do you observe?"

Show images:

- Māori pā and villages
- European settlements
- Ships in harbors
- Mission stations

#### Main Learning (25 minutes)

**Dual Timeline Activity**

Students create two parallel timelines on A3 paper:

**Māori Timeline (left side):**

- 1000+ years: Māori settlement and development
- Establishment of iwi territories
- Traditional governance systems
- Trade networks
- Population estimates (100,000+)

**European Timeline (right side):**

- 1769: Cook's arrival
- 1814: First mission station
- 1830s: Increasing settlement
- Growth in trade (flax, timber, whaling)
- British concerns about lawlessness

**Key Questions for Discussion:**

1. What were Māori priorities in 1839?
2. What were British priorities in 1839?
3. Where might these priorities conflict?
4. What solutions might both groups consider?

#### Plenary (10 minutes)

**Perspective Carousel:** Students rotate between stations answering:

- "As a Māori rangatira, my main concern is..."
- "As a British missionary, I want..."
- "As a British settler, I need..."
- "As a British government official, I must..."

### Lesson 2: Creating the Treaty - The Process

#### Starter (10 minutes)

**Treaty Word Cloud**
Display key terms and ask students to predict their importance:

- Sovereignty, Rangatiratanga, Taonga, Protection, Rights, Partnership

#### Input (20 minutes)

**The Drafting Drama**

Students role-play key figures in February 1840:

- **James Busby** (British Resident)
- **Captain William Hobson** (Naval officer)
- **Henry Williams** (Missionary/translator)
- **Various rangatira** (Māori chiefs)

Provide character cards with their motivations and concerns.

**Scene 1:** English draft being written
**Scene 2:** Translation into Te Reo Māori
**Scene 3:** Debates at Waitangi

#### Investigation Activity (15 minutes)

**Translation Detective Work**

Compare key phrases in both versions:

| English Version | Te Reo Māori Version | What's the Difference? |
|-----------------|---------------------|------------------------|
| "sovereignty" | "kāwanatanga" | |
| "undisturbed possession" | "rangatiratanga" | |
| "forests, fisheries" | "taonga katoa" | |

Students research what each term meant to each group.

#### Plenary (5 minutes)

Exit ticket: "One translation problem that could cause future issues is..."

### Lesson 3: The Signing - February 6, 1840 and Beyond

#### Hook (5 minutes)

**Critical Decision Point**
"You are a rangatira. You must decide whether to sign this document. You have 5 minutes to convince your iwi of your choice."

#### Main Activity (30 minutes)

**Signing Ceremony Investigation**

Students research different responses to the Treaty:

**Group 1: Those who signed at Waitangi (February 6)**

- Hōne Heke
- Kawiti
- Research: Why did they sign? What did they think they were agreeing to?

**Group 2: Those who signed later**

- Around 500 rangatira eventually signed
- Research: What convinced them? What concerns did they have?

**Group 3: Those who refused to sign**

- Tāraia Ngākuti (Te Waiohua)
- Many South Island iwi
- Research: Why didn't they sign? What were their alternatives?

**Group 4: Those who weren't consulted**

- Many iwi never had the opportunity
- Women leaders excluded from the process
- Research: How did this affect the Treaty's legitimacy?

#### Evidence Analysis (10 minutes)

Students examine primary sources:

- Newspaper reports from 1840
- Letters from missionaries
- Oral histories recorded later
- Government documents

#### Plenary (5 minutes)

**One Minute Paper:** "The biggest misconception about the Treaty signing is..."

---

### Lesson 4: Broken Promises - 1840-1975

#### Starter (10 minutes)

**Expectation vs Reality**
Create two columns showing what each group expected from the Treaty versus what actually happened.

#### Investigation (30 minutes)

**Case Study Rotation**

Students rotate through 4 stations examining Treaty breaches:

**Station 1: Land Confiscations (Raupatu)**

- Taranaki Wars (1860-1861)
- Waikato invasions (1863-1864)
- Amount of land confiscated: 1.6 million hectares
- Task: Map the confiscated areas and calculate the impact

**Station 2: Language Suppression**

- Education policies banning Te Reo Māori
- Impact on cultural transmission
- Personal stories from kaumātua
- Task: Interview script with someone who experienced this

**Station 3: Political Exclusion**

- Māori not granted vote until 1867 (men only)
- Separate Māori seats created
- Limited representation
- Task: Compare Māori and European political rights timeline

**Station 4: Economic Marginalization**

- Exclusion from economic opportunities
- Impact of land loss on traditional economy
- Urban migration patterns
- Task: Create infographic showing economic statistics

#### Synthesis Activity (10 minutes)

**Treaty Report Card**
Students give the Crown a "report card" for Treaty partnership from 1840-1975:

- Article 1 (Sovereignty/Kāwanatanga): Grade and evidence
- Article 2 (Rangatiratanga): Grade and evidence
- Article 3 (Equal rights): Grade and evidence

### Lesson 5: Modern Treaty Settlements

#### Hook (5 minutes)

**Settlement Headlines**
Show newspaper headlines from various Treaty settlements:

- "Tainui receives $170 million settlement"
- "Ngāi Tahu settlement reaches $170 million"
- "Crown apologizes to Tūhoe"

Ask: "What do you think these are about?"

#### Input (15 minutes)

**How Settlements Work**

Explain the process:

1. **Research and negotiation** (can take decades)
2. **Crown acknowledgment** of breaches
3. **Formal apology**
4. **Financial redress** (compensation)
5. **Cultural redress** (land, sites returned)
6. **Relationship agreements** (ongoing partnership)

#### Case Study (25 minutes)

**Ngāi Tahu Settlement Deep Dive**

Students examine this comprehensive settlement:

**Background:**

- Land confiscations and unfair purchases
- Loss of pounamu (greenstone) rights
- Mahinga kai (food gathering) areas destroyed

**Settlement Package (1998):**

- $170 million compensation
- Return of sacred sites
- Right to purchase Crown land
- Pounamu rights restored
- Ongoing relationship agreement

**Students investigate:**

- How has this affected Ngāi Tahu today?
- What businesses has the iwi developed?
- How have they used the settlement money?
- What cultural sites were returned?

#### Current Issues Discussion (5 minutes)

**Unfinished Business**

- Constitutional arrangements
- Resource management
- Treaty principles in modern law
- Different interpretations of partnership

### Lesson 6: The Treaty Today - Assessment Task

#### Assessment: Treaty Inquiry Report (50 minutes)

**Scenario:** You are a researcher for the Waitangi Tribunal. You have been asked to investigate this question:

**"To what extent has the Treaty of Waitangi been honored as a partnership between Māori and the Crown?"**

**Your report must include:**

1. **Executive Summary** (100 words)

   - Brief overview of your findings

2. **Historical Analysis** (300 words)

   - Key events showing partnership success or failure
   - Use specific examples from 1840-1975

3. **Modern Evaluation** (300 words)

   - How Treaty settlements have addressed past wrongs
   - Current examples of Treaty partnership
   - Ongoing challenges

4. **Conclusion and Recommendations** (200 words)
   - Overall assessment of Treaty partnership
   - What could be done better
   - Future considerations

**Assessment Criteria:**

- **Historical Knowledge:** Accurate use of historical facts and dates
- **Analysis:** Examines multiple perspectives and causes/consequences
- **Evidence:** Uses specific examples to support arguments
- **Communication:** Clear structure, appropriate vocabulary
- **Critical Thinking:** Evaluates Treaty partnership rather than just describing

**Extension Options:**

- Create a multimedia presentation
- Write newspaper editorials from different time periods
- Design a Treaty education resource for younger students
- Interview community members about Treaty perspectives

## Resources and Materials

### Primary Sources

- **Treaty documents:** Original English and Te Reo Māori versions
- **Newspaper archives:** 1840 reports of the signing
- **Government documents:** Colonial Office correspondence
- **Oral histories:** Recorded kaumātua accounts
- **Photographs:** Historical images from Archives New Zealand

### Secondary Sources

- **Academic articles** on Treaty interpretation
- **Waitangi Tribunal reports** (simplified excerpts)
- **Treaty settlement summaries**
- **Modern news articles** about Treaty issues

### Digital Resources

- **NZHistory.govt.nz** Treaty timeline and documents
- **Waitangi Tribunal website** for settlement information
- **Te Ara Encyclopedia** for biographical information
- **Archives New Zealand** for historical photographs

## Differentiation Strategies

### For Advanced Students

- **Extended research** into specific Treaty settlements
- **Comparative analysis** with indigenous treaties in other countries
- **Legal interpretation** of Treaty principles in modern court cases
- **Independent inquiry** into local Treaty issues

### For Students Needing Support

- **Graphic organizers** for note-taking
- **Timeline templates** with key dates provided
- **Vocabulary support** with Te Reo Māori pronunciation guides
- **Collaborative research** with stronger partners
- **Choice of presentation formats** (visual, oral, written)

### For English Language Learners

- **Bilingual resources** where available
- **Visual supports** for key concepts
- **Peer translation** support
- **Sentence frames** for written responses
- **Extra time** for processing complex historical language

---

## Cultural Considerations

### Respectful Approaches

- **Consult with local iwi** about appropriate content and perspectives
- **Include Māori voices** in all aspects of the unit
- **Address misconceptions** sensitively and factually
- **Acknowledge ongoing impact** of historical injustices
- **Celebrate positive developments** in Treaty partnership

### Te Reo Māori Integration

- **Correct pronunciation** of all Māori terms
- **Cultural context** for key concepts like rangatiratanga
- **Use of whakataukī** (proverbs) where appropriate
- **Respect for tikanga** in classroom discussions
