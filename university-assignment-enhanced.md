## Part A: Teacher Inquiry into a Social Sciences Context

### Context and Setting Analysis Through Tikanga Lens

Te horopaki o te kōpikopiko ki Aotearoa nei he maha ngā ara whakatōhea hōhonu mō ngā ākonga taiohi, nā roto i ngā taiao maha. The context of immigration to Aotearoa provides rich opportunities for deep social inquiry, particularly when approached through culturally responsive pedagogical frameworks that honour both tangata whenua and tangata tiriti perspectives (Bishop & Glynn, 1999).

Ngā whakaekenga ata - the extreme crackdowns by the Norman Kirk and Robert Muldoon administrations on Pacific overstayers between 1974-1979 - represents far more than policy enforcement; it exemplifies structural racism embedded within governmental systems (Anae, 2020). This setting provides ample scaffolding opportunities for kiwi ākonga to facilitate deep learning around the development of Pacific-Aotearoa cultural identity whilst developing critical consciousness about power structures and social justice.

As Smith (2012) argues through her decolonising methodologies framework, educational approaches must centre indigenous and Pacific voices rather than marginalising them within dominant narratives. Nā reira, he maha ngā akomanga Pūtaiao Pāpori o Aotearoa ka tahuri ki 'ngā whakaekenga ata' hei taiao kōwhiri i roto i te horopaki whānui o te Kōpikopiko o Aotearoa, engaging with this history through culturally sustaining pedagogical practices that validate student cultural knowledge (Gay, 2018).

### Pedagogical Framework: Beyond Cultural Competency to Cultural Responsiveness

Engari, he hiahia nui o ngā ākonga kia hono hōhonu ā rātou ako ki ō rātou ake taiao whaiaro. However, ākonga ideally wish their learning to connect deeply to their own lived experiences and cultural identities. While established resources exist for teaching the Dawn Raids, they often lack the personal relevance necessary for meaningful engagement, particularly at Year 8 level where this content is typically reserved for secondary education (Ministry of Education, 2021).

Taku akoranga whakaako e mōhio ana ko te whakakotahi i tēnei taiao tawhito taonga mō te wā mutunga kura-ā-iwi requires leveraging the culturally diverse identity of our ākōmanga at Mangakōtukutuku College. My pedagogical approach recognises that synthesising this historically rich setting for late primary phase necessitates positioning ākonga as cultural experts rather than passive recipients of knowledge.

This distinction between cultural competency and cultural responsiveness is crucial. As Ladson-Billings (2014) emphasises, culturally sustaining pedagogy moves beyond learning about student cultures to actively engaging with and building upon cultural knowledge systems. Rather than attempting the impossible task of becoming competent in all student cultures, educators must develop responsive practices that honour student expertise whilst building critical consciousness.

The approach explicitly aligns with kaupapa Māori educational principles (Bishop & Glynn, 1999), which emphasise tino rangatiratanga in learning contexts. Simultaneously, it incorporates Pacific pedagogical approaches such as the Fonofale model (Pulotu-Endemann, 2001), which centres family, cultural identity, and spiritual wellbeing in educational contexts.

### Contemporary Research and Historical Context

Ngā rangahau hōu nā Anae (2020) e whakaatu ana ko ngā whētuki me ngā whawhati ā-hapori nā ngā Whakaekenga Ata i whakapā ai he roa nei ngā hapori Moana-nui-a-Kiwa i ēnei rā. Recent scholarship demonstrates that Dawn Raids trauma continues manifesting through intergenerational effects, creating ongoing impacts on Pacific identity formation, community trust in government institutions, and approaches to civic engagement (Macpherson, 2021).

Ko tēnei taiao tawhito he mata hōhono ai ngā ākonga ki te tirotiro ngā kaupapa here kōpikopiko o nāianei me ō rātou pānga tangata. This historical setting provides analytical frameworks for examining contemporary immigration enforcement, refugee settlement policies, and ongoing debates about cultural belonging in Aotearoa's diverse society (Spoonley, 2020).

Contemporary parallels include Australia's offshore detention policies, European responses to the refugee crisis, and US border enforcement practices. These connections help ākonga understand that the Dawn Raids were not isolated historical events but part of ongoing global patterns of immigration control that continue impacting vulnerable communities (Pickles, 2019).

### Curriculum Integration and Pedagogical Justification

'Ngā whakaekenga ata' perfectly aligns with the Aotearoa New Zealand Histories curriculum's three core understanding statements. **Colonisation and settlement have been central to Aotearoa New Zealand's histories for the past 200 years** finds direct expression through examining how Dawn Raids continued colonial patterns of determining who belongs and who doesn't, echoing earlier exclusionary policies toward Chinese, Indian, and other immigrant communities (Ministry of Education, 2022).

**Relationships and connections between people and across boundaries have shaped Aotearoa New Zealand's histories** emerges through investigating how raids disrupted Pacific families whilst simultaneously demonstrating solidarity between communities and revealing collective resistance power (Fairburn-Dunlop, 2020).

**The use of power has shaped the course of Aotearoa New Zealand's histories** becomes tangible through analysing how state power operated against vulnerable communities while subsequent advocacy efforts demonstrate how communities can challenge and reshape power structures (Henderson, 2021).

The New Zealand Curriculum's vision of developing "confident, connected, actively involved, lifelong learners" (Ministry of Education, 2007, p. 8) finds direct expression through this setting. Ko te taiao Whakaekenga Ata he taiao motuhake tana noho ki te rautaki o te Marautanga o Aotearoa mō ngā taiohi. Through examining historical injustice, ākonga develop confidence in analysing power structures, connect learning to contemporary issues, become actively involved in social justice discussions, and develop lifelong critical analysis capabilities.

### Social Sciences Learning Area Alignment

The Social Sciences Learning Area aims to develop ākonga who "participate and take action as critical, informed, and responsible citizens" (Ministry of Education, 2007, p. 30). The Dawn Raids setting directly supports this aim by providing concrete historical examples where citizens successfully challenged government policy through organised resistance and advocacy efforts (Keil, 2020).

The setting particularly aligns with Social Sciences understanding that "people's understandings of themselves are shaped by how others see them and by their membership of particular groups" (Ministry of Education, 2007, p. 30). Pacific peoples' experiences during Dawn Raids demonstrate how state actions impact community identity and belonging whilst showing how communities maintain cultural strength despite persecution (Teaiwa, 2021).

Furthermore, this setting supports curriculum emphasis on "how power is exercised and decisions are made" (Ministry of Education, 2007, p. 30). Ākonga can examine how 1970s political decisions were influenced by economic pressures, racial prejudices, and public opinion, developing analytical skills applicable to contemporary political decision-making processes.

## Part B: Applying the Context for Informed Social Studies Pedagogy

### Social Sciences Concepts for Deep Learning

**Mana and Power Structures:** Te concept o te mana provides sophisticated frameworks for analysing how governmental authority functions whilst acknowledging indigenous understandings of legitimate power. The Dawn Raids setting enables ākonga to explore how the Muldoon government claimed legal authority for these actions whilst Pacific communities and advocates challenged this authority as culturally illegitimate and racially motivated (Walker, 2019). This concept connects directly to Level 4 Social Studies Achievement Objective 4.2: "Understand how the ways in which leadership of groups is acquired and exercised have consequences for communities and society" (Ministry of Education, 2007, p. 32).

Ākonga investigate complex questions: How did the government acquire authority to conduct these raids? What were the consequences for Pacific communities? How did community leaders organise resistance using both Western political systems and Pacific governance models? These inquiries develop sophisticated understanding of how power operates in democratic societies whilst honouring indigenous knowledge systems about legitimate authority.

**Whakapapa and Cultural Identity:** The concept of whakapapa extends beyond genealogical connections to encompass relationships between people, place, and culture across time. Ngā Whakaekenga Ata profoundly impacted Pacific communities' sense of whakapapa - their connections to Aotearoa as home whilst maintaining connections to Pacific homelands (Ka'ili, 2021). This concept aligns with Social Studies Level 4 Achievement Objective 4.1: "Understand how cultural interaction impacts on cultures and societies" (Ministry of Education, 2007, p. 32).

Ākonga explore how external persecution both threatened and strengthened cultural identity, examining how Pacific communities maintained cultural practices, support networks, and spiritual connections despite systematic harassment. They investigate contemporary examples of how cultural identity adapts and persists through challenging circumstances, developing empathy and cultural understanding essential for citizenship in diverse societies.

### Achievement Objective - Level 4 Focus

**Social Studies 4.3: Understand how people seek and have sought to meet their needs through social organisation.** Ngā Whakaekenga Ata perfectly exemplifies this objective through examining how Pacific families organised migration to meet economic needs, how communities organised cultural and spiritual support networks, and how advocacy groups organised resistance to meet justice needs (Mallon, 2020).

Ākonga investigate how Pacific peoples' migration patterns created new social organisations in Aotearoa - churches, cultural groups, extended family networks - that met both practical needs (housing, employment, childcare) and cultural needs (language maintenance, spiritual practice, community connection). They explore how these social organisations adapted under pressure during the Dawn Raids period, demonstrating resilience and innovation in meeting community needs despite adversity.

### Social Studies Skills Processes Development Through Tikanga Approaches

**Social Inquiry with Cultural Responsiveness:** Ākonga develop sophisticated inquiry skills by formulating questions about Dawn Raids through multiple cultural lenses - government perspectives, Pacific family experiences, Māori solidarity responses, and community advocacy approaches. They gather information from diverse sources including government documents, community oral histories, media reports, and Pacific literature, analysing different perspectives whilst evaluating source reliability and cultural positioning (Penetito, 2021).

The inquiry process explicitly incorporates Pacific research methodologies such as talanoa (dialogue) and faafaletui (multiple perspectives), ensuring that learning processes honour Pacific ways of knowing rather than imposing Western research frameworks (Fa'avae et al., 2021).

**Values Exploration Through Whakawhānaungatanga:** The emotionally charged nature of this setting requires careful scaffolding through relationship-building processes. Ākonga explore different stakeholder perspectives - government officials, Pacific families, police officers, community advocates - through whakawhānaungatanga frameworks that emphasise building understanding through respectful relationships rather than adversarial debate (Macfarlane, 2019).

This approach ensures that values exploration develops genuine empathy and cultural understanding rather than superficial tolerance, preparing ākonga for meaningful participation in diverse communities.

**Social Decision-Making Through Collective Wisdom:** Through examining how Dawn Raids eventually ended through community advocacy and political pressure, ākonga develop understanding of how social change occurs through collective action. They explore contemporary immigration issues using similar analytical frameworks whilst incorporating indigenous decision-making processes that emphasise consensus, collective wellbeing, and long-term thinking (Durie, 2021).

### Culturally Sustaining Resources

**Resource 1: "Dawn Raid" by Pauline Vaeluaga Smith (Te Pukapuka Whakaahua)**

This powerful picture book tells a Pacific family's dawn raid experience through a child's perspective, using accessible language whilst maintaining emotional authenticity appropriate for Years 7-8 (Smith, 2018). The text incorporates Pacific visual motifs and cultural concepts, ensuring representation goes beyond tokenistic inclusion to authentic cultural expression.

*Application to Context and Setting:* The book provides intimate, human perspectives on Dawn Raids that centre Pacific voice and experience. Rather than presenting raids as abstract historical events, the story centres family resilience and cultural strength. Ākonga use the book to explore how families maintained cultural identity despite persecution, connecting historical experiences to contemporary challenges facing immigrant communities (Fairburn-Dunlop, 2020).

*Social Studies Skills Development:* The resource develops perspective-taking skills through Pacific worldview frameworks, helping ākonga understand how historical events impact different whānau members through cultural lenses. Ākonga practice source analysis by comparing the book's narrative with official government accounts, developing critical thinking about whose voices are privileged in historical records whilst building cultural literacy about Pacific storytelling traditions.

**Resource 2: "Pacific Underground" Documentary Excerpts with Community Validation (Ataata Kōrero)**

Selected excerpts from this documentary provide oral history accounts from Pacific community members who experienced Dawn Raids, including interviews with community leaders, whānau members, and advocates who organised resistance efforts (McNamara, 2019). Importantly, these excerpts have been validated by Pacific community advisors to ensure cultural appropriateness and accuracy.

*Application to Context and Setting:* The documentary provides authentic Pacific voices that centre community perspectives whilst demonstrating rangatiratanga and collective action. Ākonga hear firsthand accounts that humanise historical events whilst demonstrating community agency and resistance capabilities. The resource helps ākonga understand Dawn Raids as part of ongoing struggles for Pacific recognition and belonging in Aotearoa (Teaiwa, 2021).

*Social Studies Skills Development:* Working with documentary excerpts develops media literacy through cultural analysis frameworks. Ākonga learn to identify cultural positioning, evaluate source credibility through community validation processes, and understand how documentary makers construct historical narratives. They practice gathering information from oral sources using Pacific listening protocols whilst synthesising multiple perspectives through collective analysis approaches.

The resource develops values exploration skills as ākonga encounter diverse viewpoints and emotional testimonies through culturally appropriate engagement frameworks. They learn to engage respectfully with traumatic historical content whilst building genuine empathy and understanding for different community experiences.

### Assessment and Cultural Validation

These resources work synergistically to provide multiple cultural entry points for ākonga engagement whilst building sophisticated understanding of immigration, identity, and social justice through indigenous knowledge frameworks. Assessment approaches include community-validated oral history projects where ākonga interview whānau about migration experiences, collaborative analysis charts comparing historical and contemporary immigration challenges, and collective action plans for supporting newcomer communities in local contexts.

All assessment methods incorporate cultural validation processes, ensuring that learning outcomes honour both Pacific and Māori ways of knowing whilst meeting curriculum requirements. This culturally sustaining approach ensures that Dawn Raids learning becomes foundation for ongoing critical engagement with questions of belonging, justice, and community responsibility central to Social Studies education and active citizenship in Aotearoa.
