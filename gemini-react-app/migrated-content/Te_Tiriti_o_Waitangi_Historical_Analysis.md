## 🌿 Cultural Foundation / Tūāpapa Tikanga

### **Core Māori Concepts for Understanding**

#### **Rangatiratanga** (Chiefly Authority/Sovereignty)

- The absolute authority of Māori chiefs over their people and territories
- Not equivalent to European concepts of sovereignty
- Includes spiritual, political, and ancestral responsibilities
- Central to Māori understanding of what they were guaranteed in the Treaty

#### **Mana** (Spiritual Power/Authority)

- The spiritual power and prestige of individuals and groups  
- Cannot be given away or surrendered
- Fundamental to Māori identity and leadership
- Challenges European assumptions about power transfer

#### **Whakapapa** (Genealogical Connections)

- Connections to ancestors, land, and spiritual realm
- Basis for Māori rights and responsibilities
- Informs how Māori understand their relationship to whenua (land)
- Cannot be understood through European property concepts

#### **Taonga** (Treasured Possessions)

- Sacred objects, resources, and knowledge
- Includes language, cultural practices, natural resources
- Has spiritual and cultural significance beyond material value
- Guaranteed protection under Article II of the Treaty

## 📖 Unit Content Structure

### **Week 1: Te Taiao - The Historical Landscape (Pre-1840)**

#### **Lesson 1: Māori Society Before European Contact**

- **Iwi and hapū social structures**
- **Economic systems and resource management**  
- **Governance through rangatiratanga**
- **Spiritual relationships with whenua**

**Key Understanding**: Māori had sophisticated governance systems for over 600 years before European arrival.

#### **Lesson 2: Early European Contact (1769-1839)**

- **James Cook and early explorers**
- **Missionaries and their influence**
- **Traders and the development of economic relationships**
- **Growing tensions and the "lawless state" narrative**

**Critical Analysis**: Examine whose perspectives defined this period as "lawless" and why.

---

### **Week 2: Te Wā o te Tiriti - The Treaty Moment (1840)**

#### **Lesson 3: The Path to Waitangi**

- **Governor Hobson's instructions from Britain**
- **James Busby's role as British Resident**
- **Māori concerns about increasing Pākehā settlement**
- **The decision to seek a treaty agreement**

**Primary Sources**:

- Hobson's instructions from Lord Normanby
- Busby's reports on the "state of the colony"
- Māori letters expressing concerns

#### **Lesson 4: Waitangi - February 5-6, 1840**

- **The debate at Waitangi**
- **Speeches by Māori chiefs**
- **William Hobson's presentation**
- **The decision-making process**

**Cultural Insight**: Understanding Māori consensus-building processes vs European expectations.

**Key Speeches to Analyze**:

- Tāmati Wāka Nene supporting the Treaty
- Tarore opposing British sovereignty
- Kawiti's warnings about loss of mana

---

### **Week 3: Ngā Rerenga Rua - The Two Versions**

#### **Lesson 5: Translation Challenges - What Was Actually Agreed?**

**Critical Comparison**:

| **English Text** | **Te Reo Māori Text** | **Significance** |
|------------------|----------------------|------------------|
| "sovereignty" | "kāwanatanga" (governance) | Māori understood giving limited governing authority, not sovereignty |
| "possession" | "tino rangatiratanga" (absolute chiefly authority) | Māori believed they retained full authority over their affairs |
| "property" | "taonga" (treasured possessions) | Includes cultural and spiritual treasures, not just material goods |

**Essential Question**: Were Māori and the Crown agreeing to the same thing?

**Historical Evidence**:

- Henry Williams' translation choices
- Māori oral histories of what they understood
- Legal interpretations over time

#### **Lesson 6: Immediate Aftermath and Early Conflicts**

- **Māori expectations vs Colonial reality**
- **Land sales and the establishment of settlements**
- **Early conflicts over authority**
- **The New Zealand Company and organized colonization**

---

### **Week 4: Ngā Hua - The Consequences and Legacy**

#### **Lesson 7: The Wars and Land Loss (1845-1872)**

- **Northern War (1845-46)**
- **Wellington and Hutt Valley conflicts**
- **Taranaki and Waikato Wars**
- **Land confiscations (raupatu)**

**Māori Perspective**: These were not "Māori Wars" but wars of colonial aggression to secure land.

**Statistical Reality**:

- 1840: Māori owned virtually all land in New Zealand
- 1900: Māori retained less than 5% of their ancestral lands

#### **Lesson 8: Te Tiriti Today - Contemporary Significance**

- **The Treaty of Waitangi Act 1975**
- **Waitangi Tribunal and Treaty settlements**
- **Contemporary debates and challenges**
- **The Treaty as a living document**

**Case Studies**:

- Ngāi Tahu Settlement (1998)
- Waikato-Tainui Settlement (1995)
- Tūhoe Settlement (2013)
