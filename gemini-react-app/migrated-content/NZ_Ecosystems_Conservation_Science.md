## 🌿 Cultural Framework / Tikanga Taiao

### **Kaitiakitanga (Environmental Guardianship)**:
- The responsibility to care for and protect the natural world
- Recognizing humans as part of nature, not separate from it
- Making decisions that consider impacts on future generations
- Understanding that environmental health affects cultural wellbeing

### **Te Taiao (The Natural World)**:
- All living and non-living things are interconnected
- Respect for the mauri (life force) in all natural things
- Traditional ecological knowledge informs conservation practices
- Sustainable use rather than exploitation of resources

### **Whakapapa (Genealogical Connections)**:
- Connections between all species and natural elements
- Understanding evolutionary relationships in NZ's unique environment
- Humans' place within the broader family of life

## 📖 Unit Content Structure

### **Week 1: Ngā Taiao - Understanding NZ Ecosystems**

#### **Lesson 1: Aotearoa's Unique Natural Heritage**
- **Why NZ ecosystems are special**: 80 million years of isolation
- **Endemic species showcase**: Tuatara, kakapo, giant weta, native frogs
- **Major ecosystem types**: Forests, wetlands, alpine, marine, grasslands
- **Kaitiakitanga connection**: Traditional Māori understanding of ecosystem relationships

**Activities**:
- Virtual ecosystem exploration using NZ nature documentaries
- Create ecosystem identification cards with both scientific and Te Reo names
- Map NZ's major ecosystems and their locations

**Key Species Focus**: 
- **Kakapo** (Strigops habroptilus) - world's only flightless parrot
- **Tuatara** (Sphenodon punctatus) - living fossil, unique to NZ
- **Kiwi** (Apteryx species) - iconic flightless bird
- **Giant Weta** (Deinacrida species) - ancient insect group

#### **Lesson 2: Forest Ecosystems - From Kauri to Beech**
- **Kauri forests**: Ancient giants and their ecosystem role
- **Native beech forests**: Red, silver, mountain, and black beech
- **Understory plants**: Ferns, mosses, epiphytes
- **Forest food webs**: From invertebrates to birds to predators

**Practical Investigation**: 
- Analyze leaf shapes and adaptations of NZ native plants
- Create food web diagrams for native forest ecosystems
- Examine how different forest layers support different species

**Cultural Connection**: 
- **Tane Mahuta** (Lord of the Forest) - largest living kauri tree
- Traditional uses of native plants by Māori
- Sustainable harvesting principles in tikanga Māori

---

### **Week 2: Ngā Raru - Threats and Challenges**

#### **Lesson 3: Introduced Species - The Invasion Impact**
- **Why invasive species are problematic**: No natural predators, rapid reproduction
- **Major plant invaders**: Gorse, wilding pines, old man's beard, Japanese knotweed
- **Animal invaders and their impacts**: Possums, stoats, rats, cats, mustelids
- **Marine invaders**: Mediterranean fanworm, sea squirts

**Case Study Deep Dive**:
**Possums in NZ**:
- Introduced from Australia in 1837 for fur trade
- Population exploded to 70+ million by 1980s
- Impact: Eat 20,000 tonnes of vegetation nightly
- Economic damage: $70+ million annually to forestry
- Disease transmission: Bovine tuberculosis to cattle

**Practical Activity**: 
- Calculate ecological impact using data (e.g., possum consumption rates)
- Research and present on one invasive species
- Design a "Wanted" poster for an invasive species

#### **Lesson 4: Habitat Loss and Climate Change**
- **Historical habitat loss**: 75% of original forest cover lost
- **Wetland destruction**: 90% of original wetlands drained or filled
- **Urban development impacts**: Habitat fragmentation
- **Climate change effects**: Shifting temperature zones, sea level rise

**Data Analysis Activity**:
- Graph historical forest cover data over time
- Analyze temperature change data for NZ regions
- Calculate habitat loss percentages for different ecosystems

**Climate Change Impacts on NZ Species**:
- Alpine plants shifting to higher elevations
- Penguin breeding ground loss from sea level rise
- Coral bleaching in northern marine areas
- Changes in food availability for native birds

---

### **Week 3: Ngā Rongoā - Conservation Solutions**

#### **Lesson 5: Conservation Success Stories**
- **Predator-proof sanctuaries**: Zealandia, Tiritiri Matangi, Kapiti Island
- **Species recovery programs**: Kakapo recovery, black robin rescue
- **Pest control innovations**: 1080 operations, trapping networks
- **Habitat restoration**: Native plant revegetation projects

**Success Story Spotlight**:
**Black Robin Recovery (Petroica traversi)**:
- 1980: Only 5 birds remained (1 breeding female - "Old Blue")
- 2021: Population over 300 birds
- Techniques: Cross-fostering, habitat management, genetic monitoring
- Result: Species saved from extinction

**Community Conservation**:
- **Forest & Bird Society**: Volunteer conservation work
- **Predator Free NZ**: National pest eradication goal by 2050
- **School conservation projects**: Native plant nurseries, bird monitoring

#### **Lesson 6: Kaitiakitanga in Action - Our Role as Guardians**
- **Traditional ecological management**: Rāhui (temporary protection), seasonal harvesting
- **Modern kaitiakitanga**: Combining traditional and scientific knowledge
- **Personal conservation actions**: What students can do
- **Future careers in conservation**: Department of Conservation, ecological research

**Action Planning Project**:
Students design a conservation action plan for their local area:
- Identify local ecosystem and its threats
- Research successful conservation strategies
- Propose specific actions for their school/community
- Present to class with justification and evidence

**Career Connections**:
- **Conservation biologist**: Research and protect native species
- **Ecological restoration specialist**: Restore damaged habitats
- **DOC ranger**: Protect national parks and reserves
- **Environmental educator**: Teach others about conservation

## 🔬 Practical Investigations / Rangahau

### **Investigation 1: Local Ecosystem Survey**
Students conduct a biodiversity survey of their school grounds:
- **Method**: Quadrat sampling for plants, bird observations, invertebrate sampling
- **Data collection**: Species identification, abundance counts, habitat descriptions
- **Analysis**: Calculate biodiversity indices, compare different areas
- **Report**: Scientific format with methods, results, discussion

### **Investigation 2: Invasive Species Mapping**
Create a map of invasive species in the local area:
- **Field work**: GPS mapping of invasive plant locations
- **Research**: Identify species and their origins
- **Impact assessment**: Document native species displacement
- **Recommendations**: Suggest control or eradication methods

### **Investigation 3: Conservation Effectiveness Study**
Analyze the success of a local conservation project:
- **Data gathering**: Before/after photos, species counts, scientific reports
- **Interview**: Conservation workers or volunteers
- **Evaluation**: Assess project success against stated goals
- **Presentation**: Create a documentary-style video report

## 📊 Assessment Framework / Aromatawai

### **Assessment 1: Ecosystem Portfolio (Week 1)**
Students create a comprehensive portfolio on one NZ ecosystem:
- **Scientific description**: Physical characteristics, climate, soil
- **Species profiles**: Native plants and animals with adaptations
- **Cultural significance**: Traditional Māori uses and beliefs
- **Current status**: Health, threats, protection level

### **Assessment 2: Conservation Debate (Week 2)**
Students participate in a structured debate on conservation priorities:
- **Research**: Evidence for different conservation strategies
- **Argumentation**: Present scientific evidence persuasively
- **Critical thinking**: Evaluate opposing viewpoints
- **Communication**: Use appropriate scientific and cultural terminology

### **Assessment 3: Action Plan Project (Week 3)**
Students design and present a local conservation action plan:
- **Problem identification**: Clear description of local environmental issue
- **Scientific justification**: Evidence-based reasoning for action
- **Implementation strategy**: Realistic timeline and resource requirements
- **Evaluation methods**: How to measure success
