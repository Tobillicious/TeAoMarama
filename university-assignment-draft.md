## Executive Summary

This assignment examines how the historical Dawn Raids (1974-1979) provide a powerful lens for Year 8 ākonga to understand contemporary immigration challenges in Aotearoa New Zealand and globally. Through culturally responsive pedagogy and trauma-informed practice, this setting enables deep engagement with social studies concepts while honoring Pacific voices and experiences. The analysis demonstrates how historical inquiry can foster critical citizenship and social justice awareness in young learners.

---

## Part A: Teacher Inquiry into a Social Sciences Context

### Context and Setting Analysis: The Dawn Raids as Pedagogical Foundation

The context of immigration to Aotearoa New Zealand provides rich avenues of social inquiry for young ākonga, offering multiple settings that inherently require sophisticated social studies skills processes. The Dawn Raids—the systematic crackdowns by the Norman Kirk and Robert Muldoon administrations on Pacific overstayers between 1974-1979—represent a particularly potent setting for deep learning around the development of shared pan-Pacific cultural identity in Aotearoa.

This historical setting offers exceptional scaffolding opportunities for kiwi ākonga to develop critical thinking skills while exploring complex social dynamics. The Dawn Raids setting is frequently utilized in New Zealand Social Studies classrooms due to its rich educational potential and the availability of well-developed resources. However, the challenge lies in making this historical event personally relevant to Year 8 ākonga, who may struggle to connect with events that occurred before their parents were born.

**Pedagogical Innovation:** To address this challenge, I propose connecting the Dawn Raids setting to a comprehensive Term 1 unit on culture and identity, extending the historical analysis to contemporary applications. This approach involves:

- Examining how similar policies might manifest today
- Comparing historical events to current global immigration challenges
- Leveraging ākonga's personal migration stories and cultural knowledge
- Creating project-based inquiries that build on existing cultural expertise

### Contemporary Relevance and Research Foundations: Bridging Past and Present

**Intergenerational Trauma and Contemporary Impact**

Research by Anae (2020, p. 45), building on Waikato's tradition of Pacific education scholarship, demonstrates that the trauma and social fractures caused by the Dawn Raids continue to impact Pacific communities today, creating intergenerational effects that manifest in contemporary immigration discourse. This historical setting provides a crucial lens through which ākonga can examine current immigration policies and their human impacts, developing critical thinking skills essential for active citizenship in Aotearoa's diverse society.

The Ministry of Social Development's 2021 report on Pacific wellbeing indicates that understanding historical injustices like the Dawn Raids is crucial for addressing contemporary inequities. When ākonga explore this setting, they develop empathy and understanding that directly connects to current debates about refugee settlement, immigration quotas, and cultural integration policies.

**Systemic Racism and Institutional Power**

Anae's foundational research (2020, pp. 67-72) reveals that the Dawn Raids were not merely policy implementations but represented systematic racial profiling that targeted Pacific peoples specifically. The random nature of raids, conducted predominantly in areas with high Pacific populations, demonstrates how immigration enforcement can become a tool of institutional racism (Anae, 2020). This historical analysis provides ākonga with frameworks for examining contemporary immigration enforcement practices globally.

**Contemporary Parallels and Global Context**

The Dawn Raids find echoes in current global immigration challenges:

- Australia's offshore detention policies and "Pacific Solution"
- United States border enforcement practices and family separation policies
- European responses to refugee crises and migration flows
- New Zealand's own contemporary immigration debates and policy discussions

### Pedagogical Approach: Culturally Responsive and Trauma-Informed Practice

**University of Waikato Pedagogical Framework Integration**

My pedagogical approach recognises that synthesizing this historically rich setting for late primary phase requires leveraging the culturally diverse identity of the ākōmanga, positioning ākonga as cultural experts while building new understandings. This approach aligns with core University of Waikato pedagogical frameworks:

1. **University of Waikato's foundational work by Bishop and Glynn (1999, pp. 78-82) on kaupapa Māori pedagogy:** Emphasizing tino rangatiratanga (self-determination) in learning
2. **Waikato's Linda Tuhiwai Smith's (2012, pp. 144-156) decolonising methodology:** Ensuring Pacific and Māori voices are centered in historical narratives
3. **Te Kotahitanga Framework:** University of Waikato's Bishop et al. (2007) culturally responsive pedagogy research emphasising effective teacher-student relationships in diverse classrooms
4. **Trauma-Informed Pedagogy:** Recognizing the emotional weight of historical injustices and their contemporary impacts

**Cultural Responsiveness vs. Cultural Competency**

This approach draws on University of Waikato's Te Kotahitanga research, which distinguishes between culturally responsive pedagogy and cultural competency. Following Bishop et al.'s (2007) findings, rather than attempting to master all possible cultural knowledge, we recognise ākonga as experts in their own cultural domains and defer to their expertise. This distinction is crucial for authentic engagement with diverse learners and prevents the "impossible task" of cultural mastery, while building the caring, learning relationships central to Te Kotahitanga.

**Scaffolding and Inquiry Development**

The pedagogical approach involves:

- Building inquiry skills through guided questioning
- Creating project-based learning opportunities
- Developing personal relevance through family migration stories
- Fostering critical analysis of contemporary immigration policies
- Encouraging social action and community engagement

### Curriculum Integration and Vision Alignment: Comprehensive Framework

**Aotearoa New Zealand Histories Curriculum Alignment**

The Dawn Raids setting addresses all three non-phase-specific 'Do' statements:

- **Identifying and exploring historical relationships:** Examining connections between past and present immigration policies
- **Identifying sources and perspectives:** Analyzing multiple viewpoints on historical events
- **Interpreting past experiences, decisions, and actions:** Understanding the human impact of policy decisions

**New Zealand Curriculum Vision Connection**

The setting directly supports the vision of young people as "confident, connected, actively involved, lifelong learners" (Ministry of Education, 2007, p. 8):

- **Confident:** Developing confidence in analysing power structures and social justice issues
- **Connected:** Building connections between historical events and contemporary challenges
- **Actively involved:** Engaging in discussions about social justice and community responsibility
- **Lifelong learners:** Developing skills in critical analysis of government policy and social change

**Big Ideas Integration**

The three "Big Ideas" from the Aotearoa New Zealand Histories curriculum find direct expression:

1. **Colonisation and settlement have been central to Aotearoa New Zealand's histories for the past 200 years.** The Dawn Raids represent a continuation of colonial power structures, where the state determined who belonged and who didn't, echoing earlier exclusionary policies toward Chinese, Indian, and other immigrant communities.

2. **Relationships and connections between people and across boundaries have shaped the course of Aotearoa New Zealand's histories.** The raids disrupted Pacific communities and families, but also demonstrated solidarity between communities and revealed the power of collective resistance and advocacy.

3. **The course of Aotearoa New Zealand's histories has been shaped by the use of power.** The raids exemplify how state power can be wielded against vulnerable communities, while subsequent advocacy and eventual government apology demonstrate how communities can challenge and reshape power structures.

### Social Sciences Learning Area Alignment: Deep Conceptual Integration

**Citizenship and Social Action**

The Social Sciences Learning Area aims to develop ākonga who "participate and take action as critical, informed, and responsible citizens" (Ministry of Education, 2007, p. 30). The Dawn Raids setting directly supports this aim by providing a concrete historical example where citizens successfully challenged government policy through organised resistance and advocacy.

**Identity and Belonging**

The setting particularly aligns with the understanding that "people's understandings of themselves are shaped by how others see them and by their membership of particular groups" (Ministry of Education, 2007, p. 30). Pacific peoples' experiences during the Dawn Raids demonstrate how state actions can impact community identity and belonging, while also showing how communities can maintain cultural strength despite persecution.

**Power and Decision-Making**

This setting supports the curriculum's emphasis on "how power is exercised and decisions are made" (Ministry of Education, 2007, p. 30). Ākonga can examine how political decisions in the 1970s were influenced by economic pressures, racial prejudices, and public opinion, developing skills in analysing contemporary political decision-making processes.

### Connecting to Contemporary Global Context: Personal and Universal Relevance

**Personalizing the Historical Setting**

While the Dawn Raids context is rich and culturally relevant to tauira at Mangakōtukutuku College, existing resources are often impersonal and designed for mass appeal. To address this, the approach involves:

- Tying historical events to personal migration stories of ākonga
- Creating family history projects that connect individual experiences to broader historical narratives
- Developing community-based inquiry projects that honor local knowledge and experiences
- Encouraging ākonga to share their cultural expertise and family stories

**Global Parallels and Contemporary Connections**

Contemporary parallels exist globally, providing opportunities for comparative analysis:

- **Australia:** Offshore detention policies and the "Pacific Solution"
- **United States:** Border enforcement practices and family separation policies
- **Europe:** Refugee crisis responses and migration control measures
- **New Zealand:** Current immigration debates and policy discussions

These connections help ākonga understand that the Dawn Raids were not an isolated historical event but part of ongoing global patterns of immigration control that continue to impact vulnerable communities today.

## Part B: Applying the Context for Informed Social Studies Pedagogy

### Social Sciences Concepts Exploration: Deep Conceptual Analysis

**Power and Authority: Understanding State Power and Resistance**

The Dawn Raids setting provides an exceptional opportunity to explore how governmental power and authority function in practice. Ākonga can examine how the Muldoon government justified these actions as necessary law enforcement while Pacific communities and advocates challenged this authority as racially motivated persecution.

**Key Inquiry Questions:**

- How did the government acquire the authority to conduct these raids?
- What were the consequences for Pacific communities?
- How did community leaders respond and organise resistance?
- What role did media and public opinion play in shaping policy?

This concept connects directly to Level 4 learning, where students should "understand how the ways in which leadership of groups is acquired and exercised have consequences for communities and society" (Ministry of Education, 2007, p. 32).

**Identity, Culture, and Organisation: Cultural Resilience and Community Strength**

This concept emerges powerfully through examining how the Dawn Raids impacted Pacific communities' sense of belonging and cultural identity in Aotearoa. Ākonga can explore how external persecution can both threaten and strengthen cultural identity, examining how Pacific communities maintained cultural practices and support networks despite harassment.

**Key Learning Opportunities:**

- How communities maintain cultural practices under pressure
- The role of cultural organizations in resistance and advocacy
- How identity is shaped by both internal cultural values and external perceptions
- The power of collective action and community solidarity

The concept aligns with Level 4 expectations that students "understand how groups make and implement rules and laws" and "understand how cultural interaction impacts on cultures and societies" (Ministry of Education, 2007, p. 32).

### Achievement Objective - Level 4: Comprehensive Skill Development

**Social Studies 4.1: Understand how exploration and innovation create opportunities and challenges for people, places, and environments.**

The Dawn Raids setting fits this objective by examining how Pacific migration to Aotearoa (as exploration and innovation in seeking economic opportunities) created both opportunities for families and communities, and challenges that manifested in governmental backlash and community persecution.

**Learning Outcomes:**

- Understanding complex cause-and-effect relationships in social contexts
- Developing empathy for diverse perspectives and experiences
- Analyzing how economic and social factors influence policy decisions
- Examining the human impact of political and social change

### Social Studies Skills Processes Development: Comprehensive Skill Building

**Social Inquiry: From Simple Questions to Complex Analysis**

Students will develop sophisticated inquiry skills through a progression of questioning:

**Level 1: Factual Questions**

- What were the Dawn Raids?
- When did they occur?
- Who was affected?

**Level 2: Analytical Questions**

- Why did the government implement these policies?
- How did Pacific communities respond?
- What were the immediate and long-term consequences?

**Level 3: Evaluative Questions**

- Were the Dawn Raids justified?
- How do we evaluate the fairness of immigration policies?
- What lessons can we learn for contemporary immigration debates?

**Level 4: Action-Oriented Questions**

- How can we apply historical lessons to current challenges?
- What actions can we take to support immigrant communities?
- How can we promote social justice in our own communities?

**Values Exploration: Navigating Complex Ethical Terrain**

The emotionally charged nature of this setting requires careful scaffolding of values exploration. Students will examine different stakeholder perspectives:

- **Government officials:** Policy implementation and legal frameworks
- **Pacific families:** Personal experiences and community impact
- **Police officers:** Law enforcement and personal conscience
- **Community advocates:** Resistance and social change
- **Media representatives:** Public perception and narrative construction

**Social Decision-Making: From Analysis to Action**

Through examining how the Dawn Raids were eventually ended through community advocacy and political pressure, ākonga develop understanding of how social change occurs. They can explore contemporary immigration issues using similar analytical frameworks, developing skills in proposing and evaluating social action.

### Child-Centered Resources: Multi-Modal Learning Approaches

**Resource 1: "Dawn Raid" by Pauline Vaeluaga Smith (Picture Book)**

This powerful picture book tells the story of a Pacific family experiencing a dawn raid through a child's perspective. The text uses simple, accessible language while conveying the fear and confusion experienced by families, making it appropriate for Year 7-8 students while maintaining emotional authenticity.

**Application to Context and Setting:**

- Provides intimate, human perspective on historical events
- Centers family experience and cultural resilience
- Demonstrates how families maintain cultural identity despite persecution
- Offers entry point for discussing personal and family experiences

**Social Studies Skills Development:**

- **Perspective-taking:** Understanding events from a child's viewpoint
- **Source analysis:** Comparing narrative with official accounts
- **Critical thinking:** Examining whose voices are privileged in historical records
- **Empathy development:** Connecting with personal and family experiences

**Resource 2: "Pacific Underground" Documentary Excerpts (Audio-Visual Resource)**

Selected excerpts from this documentary provide oral history accounts from Pacific community members who experienced the Dawn Raids. The resource includes interviews with community leaders, family members, and advocates who organised resistance efforts.

**Application to Context and Setting:**

- Provides authentic community voices and perspectives
- Centers Pacific narratives and experiences
- Demonstrates community agency and resistance
- Connects historical events to ongoing struggles for rights and recognition

**Social Studies Skills Development:**

- **Media literacy:** Identifying bias and evaluating credibility
- **Source evaluation:** Understanding how documentaries construct narratives
- **Oral history analysis:** Gathering information from personal testimonies
- **Perspective synthesis:** Combining multiple viewpoints into coherent analysis
- **Values exploration:** Engaging respectfully with traumatic content

**Resource 3: Digital Interactive Timeline (Technology-Enhanced Learning)**

A digital timeline incorporating primary sources, oral histories, and contemporary connections allows ākonga to explore the Dawn Raids chronologically while making connections to current events.

**Application to Context and Setting:**

- Provides chronological framework for understanding events
- Enables exploration of cause-and-effect relationships
- Facilitates comparison with contemporary immigration issues
- Supports differentiated learning through multiple entry points

**Social Studies Skills Development:**

- **Chronological thinking:** Understanding historical sequence and context
- **Digital literacy:** Navigating and evaluating online resources
- **Comparative analysis:** Drawing parallels between past and present
- **Independent inquiry:** Self-directed exploration of historical topics

### Integration and Assessment Opportunities: Comprehensive Evaluation

**Assessment Strategy: Multi-Modal and Culturally Responsive**

**Formative Assessment Opportunities:**

- **Inquiry Journals:** Regular reflections on learning and personal connections
- **Discussion Participation:** Engagement in class discussions and group work
- **Source Analysis:** Evaluation of historical documents and contemporary media
- **Peer Feedback:** Collaborative learning and mutual support

**Summative Assessment Options:**

**Option 1: Personal Migration Story Project**
Students research and present their own family's migration story, connecting personal experiences to broader historical and contemporary immigration themes.

**Option 2: Contemporary Immigration Analysis**
Students analyze a current immigration issue (local, national, or global) using frameworks developed through studying the Dawn Raids.

**Option 3: Social Action Project**
Students design and implement a project to support immigrant communities in their local area, applying historical lessons to contemporary action.

**Option 4: Comparative Analysis**
Students create a comparison chart or presentation examining similarities and differences between the Dawn Raids and contemporary immigration challenges.

**Assessment Criteria:**

- **Historical Understanding:** Accurate comprehension of historical events and context
- **Critical Thinking:** Analysis of multiple perspectives and complex issues
- **Personal Connection:** Meaningful engagement with personal and cultural relevance
- **Social Action:** Application of learning to contemporary challenges
- **Cultural Responsiveness:** Respectful engagement with diverse perspectives and experiences

### Pedagogical Innovation: Technology Integration and Cultural Enhancement

**Digital Storytelling and Cultural Expression**

Incorporating digital storytelling tools allows ākonga to create multimedia presentations that honor their cultural backgrounds while demonstrating historical understanding. This approach:

- Respects diverse learning styles and cultural expression preferences
- Enables creative engagement with historical content
- Provides opportunities for cultural sharing and mutual learning
- Develops digital literacy skills essential for contemporary citizenship

**Community Engagement and Real-World Application**

The pedagogical approach extends beyond the classroom through:

- **Family Interview Projects:** Ākonga interview family members about migration experiences
- **Community Guest Speakers:** Pacific community members share personal stories and perspectives
- **Local Immigration Advocacy:** Connecting with local organizations supporting immigrant communities
- **Cultural Celebration Events:** Honoring Pacific cultures and contributions to Aotearoa

**Differentiated Instruction and Cultural Responsiveness**

The approach recognises diverse learning needs and cultural backgrounds through:

- **Multiple Entry Points:** Various ways to engage with historical content
- **Cultural Validation:** Honoring ākonga's cultural knowledge and experiences
- **Flexible Grouping:** Opportunities for both individual and collaborative learning
- **Choice in Assessment:** Multiple ways to demonstrate understanding and learning

## Conclusion: Building Critical Citizenship Through Historical Inquiry

This pedagogical approach ensures that learning about the Dawn Raids becomes a foundation for ongoing critical engagement with questions of belonging, justice, and community responsibility that are central to Social Studies education and active citizenship in Aotearoa New Zealand.

Through culturally responsive pedagogy, trauma-informed practice, and comprehensive skill development, ākonga develop the knowledge, skills, and dispositions necessary for active participation in a diverse and democratic society. The Dawn Raids setting provides a powerful lens for understanding contemporary immigration challenges while honoring Pacific voices and experiences.

The approach demonstrates how historical inquiry can foster critical citizenship, social justice awareness, and cultural understanding in young learners, preparing them to engage thoughtfully and responsibly with the complex social issues they will encounter throughout their lives.
