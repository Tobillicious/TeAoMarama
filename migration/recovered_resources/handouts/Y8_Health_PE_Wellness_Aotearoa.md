## Rich Text Resource: Health and Wellness in Aotearoa

### 🌿 **Te Whare Tapa Whā - The Four Pillars of Māori Health**

**Foundation Concept**: Mason Durie's model representing health as a wharenui (meeting house) with four essential walls

**Taha Tinana (Physical Health)**:
- **Definition**: Physical capacity and bodily functions
- **Elements**: Fitness, nutrition, sleep, hygiene, safety from harm
- **Māori perspective**: Body as temple/sacred vessel requiring care and respect
- **Contemporary focus**: Exercise, balanced diet, medical care, injury prevention
- **Cultural practices**: Traditional foods (kai), physical activities (waka ama, ki-o-rahi)

**Taha Hinengaro (Mental/Emotional Health)**:
- **Definition**: Thoughts, feelings, emotions, and psychological wellbeing
- **Elements**: Self-esteem, emotional regulation, stress management, mental clarity
- **Māori perspective**: Mind and emotions as interconnected with spiritual and social health
- **Contemporary focus**: Mental health awareness, resilience, positive thinking, seeking help
- **Cultural practices**: Whakatōhea (storytelling), waiata (songs), meditation, counseling

**Taha Whānau (Social Health)**:
- **Definition**: Relationships, social connections, and sense of belonging
- **Elements**: Family bonds, friendships, community involvement, cultural identity
- **Māori perspective**: Collective wellbeing, whakapapa (genealogy), iwi connections
- **Contemporary focus**: Social skills, teamwork, communication, cultural diversity appreciation
- **Cultural practices**: Hui (gatherings), pōwhiri (welcomes), collective activities, mentorship

**Taha Wairua (Spiritual Health)**:
- **Definition**: Connection to beliefs, values, and sense of purpose
- **Elements**: Personal values, life meaning, connection to nature, faith traditions
- **Māori perspective**: Whakapapa to land, ancestors, and spiritual realm
- **Contemporary focus**: Personal beliefs, ethical living, environmental connection, mindfulness
- **Cultural practices**: Karakia (prayers), connection to whenua (land), spiritual reflection

### 📊 **New Zealand Health Statistics and Trends (2023)**

**Overall Health Indicators**:
- **Life expectancy**: 82.1 years (average), 80.1 years (males), 84.1 years (females)
- **Healthy life expectancy**: 72.8 years (time lived in good health)
- **Infant mortality rate**: 4.2 per 1,000 births (lowest in NZ history)
- **Healthcare spending**: $25.8 billion (10.1% of GDP)

**Youth Health Statistics** (12-17 years):
- **Self-reported good health**: 87.3% of young people
- **Regular physical activity**: 68.2% meet recommended guidelines (60+ minutes daily)
- **Mental health concerns**: 23.7% report anxiety or depression symptoms
- **Adequate sleep**: 45.6% get recommended 8-10 hours on school nights
- **Healthy diet**: 34.9% eat 5+ servings of fruits/vegetables daily

**Physical Activity Participation** (Youth 13-17):
- **Organized sports**: 72.4% participate in at least one sport
- **Most popular activities**: 
  1. Swimming (31.2%)
  2. Rugby (18.7% boys, 8.3% girls)
  3. Netball (28.4% girls, 2.1% boys)
  4. Basketball (15.8%)
  5. Football/Soccer (14.6%)
- **Outdoor recreation**: 84.3% participate in outdoor activities monthly
- **Screen time**: Average 6.8 hours daily (above recommended 2 hours)

**Mental Health and Wellbeing Data**:
- **Stress levels**: 34.2% of teens report high stress (school, social, family pressures)
- **Social connection**: 78.9% feel connected to their school community
- **Cultural identity**: 89.1% feel positive about their cultural background
- **Help-seeking**: 67.3% know where to get mental health support
- **Resilience factors**: Strong family relationships (82.4%), meaningful friendships (79.6%)

### 🏃‍♀️ **Physical Activity and Sport in New Zealand**

**National Physical Activity Guidelines** (Ages 12-17):
- **Recommended**: 60+ minutes moderate-to-vigorous activity daily
- **Muscle strengthening**: 3+ days per week
- **Bone strengthening**: 3+ days per week
- **Screen time limit**: Maximum 2 hours recreational screen time daily

**Traditional Māori Physical Activities**:
- **Ki-o-rahi**: Traditional ball game developing agility, teamwork, strategy
- **Poi**: Rhythmic activity improving coordination, balance, cultural connection
- **Waka ama**: Outrigger canoeing building strength, endurance, water safety
- **Haka**: Cultural performance enhancing fitness, cultural pride, group unity
- **Mau rākau**: Traditional martial arts developing strength, discipline, cultural knowledge

**Contemporary NZ Sport Participation Rates**:
- **Rugby**: 156,000 registered players (most popular team sport)
- **Netball**: 140,000 registered players (highest female participation)
- **Football**: 95,000 registered players (fastest growing)
- **Cricket**: 78,000 registered players (summer sport leader)
- **Basketball**: 65,000 registered players (urban popularity)

**Outdoor Recreation Statistics**:
- **Hiking/tramping**: 68% of New Zealanders participate annually
- **Swimming**: 58% participate in ocean, lake, or river swimming
- **Cycling**: 47% participate in recreational cycling
- **Water sports**: 34% participate in kayaking, surfing, sailing
- **Adventure sports**: 23% participate in rock climbing, bungee, skydiving

### 🥗 **Nutrition and Food Culture in Aotearoa**

**New Zealand Dietary Guidelines** (Adolescents):
- **Vegetables and fruits**: 5-6 servings daily (variety of colors)
- **Whole grains**: 6-8 servings daily (brown rice, wholemeal bread)
- **Protein**: 2-3 servings daily (lean meat, fish, legumes, eggs)
- **Dairy**: 3-4 servings daily (milk, cheese, yogurt)
- **Healthy fats**: Nuts, seeds, avocado, olive oil
- **Water**: 8-10 glasses daily, limit sugary drinks

**Traditional Māori Kai (Food) Practices**:
- **Seasonal eating**: Foods harvested according to natural cycles
- **Sustainable practices**: Taking only what's needed, preserving resources
- **Communal preparation**: Shared cooking and eating strengthening social bonds
- **Traditional foods**: Kūmara, paua, kina, watercress, puha, hangi cooking
- **Food as medicine**: Understanding healing properties of native plants

**Current Youth Nutrition Challenges**:
- **Processed food consumption**: 47.3% of calories from ultra-processed foods
- **Sugar intake**: Average 32.7g daily (exceeds WHO recommendations)
- **Fast food frequency**: 23.4% eat takeaways 3+ times weekly
- **Breakfast skipping**: 18.6% skip breakfast regularly
- **Food insecurity**: 12.8% of households experience food stress

**School Food Environment**:
- **School lunch programs**: 34.2% of schools provide free healthy lunches
- **Canteen policies**: 78.9% have healthy food policies
- **Water access**: 95.4% provide free drinking water
- **Food education**: 67.1% include nutrition education in curriculum
- **Garden-to-table**: 23.7% have school gardens for food education

## Three Ways to Use This Resource

### 🏠 **Activity 1: Te Whare Tapa Whā Personal Assessment**
**Time**: 45 minutes | **Materials**: Assessment sheets, reflection journals, goal-setting templates

**Individual Wellness Audit**:

**Step 1 - Four Pillars Self-Assessment** (20 minutes):
Using a 1-10 scale, students rate themselves on each dimension:

**Taha Tinana (Physical Health)**:
- Sleep quality and duration (8-10 hours nightly)
- Physical fitness and regular exercise
- Nutrition and hydration habits
- Personal hygiene and health care
- Safety awareness and injury prevention

**Taha Hinengaro (Mental/Emotional Health)**:
- Stress management and coping strategies
- Emotional regulation and expression
- Self-confidence and positive self-talk
- Learning and mental challenges
- Seeking help when needed

**Taha Whānau (Social Health)**:
- Family relationships and communication
- Friendships and peer connections
- School community involvement
- Cultural identity and pride
- Helping others and teamwork

**Taha Wairua (Spiritual Health)**:
- Connection to personal values and beliefs
- Relationship with nature and environment
- Sense of purpose and meaning
- Respect for cultural traditions
- Mindfulness and reflection practices

**Step 2 - Balance Analysis** (15 minutes):
- **Visual representation**: Create a drawing of wharenui showing relative strength of each wall
- **Interconnection mapping**: Draw arrows showing how dimensions affect each other
- **Identify patterns**: Which dimensions are strongest/weakest? How do they connect?

**Step 3 - Goal Setting** (10 minutes):
- **Priority area**: Choose one dimension needing most attention
- **SMART goals**: Set specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, time-bound goals
- **Action planning**: List 3 concrete steps for improvement
- **Support system**: Identify people/resources to help achieve goals

**Reflection Questions**:
- How does the Māori concept of hauora differ from other health models you know?
- Which dimension affects the others most strongly in your life?
- How might improving one area create positive changes in others?

### 🏃‍♀️ **Activity 2: Design a Cultural Physical Activity Program**
**Time**: 50 minutes | **Materials**: Activity research, planning templates, presentation materials

**Program Development Challenge**:

**Phase 1 - Cultural Activity Research** (15 minutes):
Students choose one traditional activity to investigate:
- **Ki-o-rahi**: Rules, skills, fitness benefits, cultural significance
- **Waka ama**: Technique, teamwork, water safety, environmental connection
- **Poi**: Coordination, rhythm, cultural stories, performance aspects
- **Traditional games**: From other Pacific or cultural backgrounds

**Research Requirements**:
- **Historical context**: Origins and cultural significance
- **Physical benefits**: Fitness components developed (strength, endurance, flexibility, coordination)
- **Social elements**: Teamwork, communication, cultural learning
- **Equipment needed**: Traditional vs. modern adaptations
- **Safety considerations**: Risk management and supervision needs

**Phase 2 - Program Design** (20 minutes):
Create a 6-week program plan including:

**Week-by-week progression**:
- Week 1-2: Basic skills and cultural introduction
- Week 3-4: Skill development and fitness building
- Week 5-6: Advanced techniques and cultural performance/competition

**Session structure** (60 minutes per session):
- **Karakia/opening** (5 minutes): Respectful beginning with cultural acknowledgment
- **Warm-up** (10 minutes): Activity-specific preparation
- **Skill development** (25 minutes): Progressive teaching of techniques
- **Fitness component** (15 minutes): Conditioning specific to activity
- **Cool-down and reflection** (5 minutes): Physical recovery and cultural discussion

**Assessment criteria**:
- **Participation**: Engagement and effort in all activities
- **Skill development**: Progress in technical abilities
- **Cultural understanding**: Respect and knowledge of cultural significance
- **Social cooperation**: Teamwork and helping others learn

**Phase 3 - Presentation** (15 minutes):
**Program pitch**: Students present their 6-week program to class as if proposing to school leadership
- **Activity demonstration**: Teach one basic skill to class
- **Benefits explanation**: Physical, social, and cultural benefits
- **Implementation plan**: How to start program at school
- **Budget and resources**: Realistic planning for equipment and space

**Extension**: Contact local cultural groups or sports clubs to learn about authentic programs and community connections.

### 📊 **Activity 3: New Zealand Youth Health Investigation**
**Time**: 60 minutes | **Materials**: Health statistics, research access, analysis tools

**Data Analysis and Health Promotion Project**:

**Investigation Options** (Choose one focus area):

**Option A - Physical Activity Trends**:
**Research Question**: "How can schools increase physical activity among New Zealand teenagers?"

**Data Analysis Tasks**:
1. **Current participation rates**: Compare NZ youth activity levels with international standards
2. **Barrier identification**: Research main obstacles to youth physical activity
3. **Successful interventions**: Find examples of effective school-based programs
4. **Cost-benefit analysis**: Calculate health and academic benefits of increased activity

**Evidence Collection**:
- **Statistics**: Use provided NZ health data to identify trends and patterns
- **Case studies**: Research successful school programs (locally and internationally)
- **Expert opinions**: Find quotes from health professionals, educators, sport scientists
- **Student voice**: Survey classmates about activity preferences and barriers

**Option B - Mental Health and Wellbeing**:
**Research Question**: "What factors most strongly support teenage mental health in New Zealand?"

**Investigation Areas**:
1. **Protective factors**: Family, school, community, and cultural connections
2. **Risk factors**: Academic pressure, social media, isolation, discrimination
3. **Help-seeking patterns**: When and how teenagers access mental health support
4. **Cultural approaches**: How different communities approach mental health and wellbeing

**Option C - Nutrition and Food Security**:
**Research Question**: "How can New Zealand ensure all teenagers have access to healthy food?"

**Analysis Focus**:
1. **Food accessibility**: Geographic, economic, and cultural barriers to healthy eating
2. **School food environments**: Impact of school meal programs and canteen policies
3. **Traditional food systems**: Role of cultural foods in health and identity
4. **Sustainable eating**: Environmental impact of food choices

**Project Deliverables** (Choose one):
- **Policy proposal**: 2-page recommendation for government or school policy
- **Health promotion campaign**: Posters, social media content, and presentation for younger students
- **Community action plan**: Specific steps for improving health in local area
- **Comparative analysis**: New Zealand vs. similar countries (Australia, Canada, Nordic countries)

**Assessment Criteria**:
- **Data literacy**: Accurate interpretation of health statistics and research
- **Critical thinking**: Analysis of multiple perspectives and complex factors
- **Cultural responsiveness**: Inclusion of diverse cultural approaches to health
- **Practical application**: Realistic and evidence-based recommendations

## Safety & Ethical Considerations

### **Physical Activity Safety**:
⚠️ **Essential Guidelines**:
- **Medical clearance**: Awareness of individual health conditions and limitations
- **Progressive training**: Gradual increase in activity intensity and duration
- **Environmental safety**: Weather conditions, equipment checks, supervision ratios
- **Cultural protocols**: Respectful participation in traditional activities

### **Health Information Privacy**:
- **Confidentiality**: Personal health information remains private
- **Voluntary disclosure**: No pressure to share sensitive health details
- **Professional boundaries**: Clear limits on health advice and support
- **Referral protocols**: Appropriate responses to serious health concerns

### **Cultural Safety in Health Education**:
- **Respectful representation**: Accurate portrayal of cultural health practices
- **Inclusive language**: Avoiding stereotypes and generalizations
- **Community consultation**: Involvement of cultural advisors and elders
- **Safe disclosure**: Creating supportive environment for sharing cultural practices
