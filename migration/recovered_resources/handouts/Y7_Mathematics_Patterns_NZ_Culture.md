*Discovering mathematical patterns through Aotearoa's cultural heritage*

## Rich Text Resource: Mathematical Patterns in Aotearoa

### 🌀 **Koru Patterns - The Mathematics of Growth**

**The Golden Spiral in Nature and Culture**
- **Koru shape**: Based on unfurling silver fern frond
- **Mathematical relationship**: Follows Fibonacci sequence (1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13...)
- **Growth ratio**: Each turn is approximately 1.618 times larger (Golden Ratio)
- **Cultural significance**: Represents new life, growth, strength, and peace

**Koru Measurements in Traditional Carving**:
- Small koru diameter: 2cm
- Medium koru diameter: 3.2cm (2 × 1.6)
- Large koru diameter: 5.2cm (3.2 × 1.625)
- Extra large koru diameter: 8.4cm (5.2 × 1.615)

### 🔺 **Tukutuku Panel Patterns**

**Traditional Geometric Sequences in Māori Weaving**

**Pūhoro Pattern** (Speed/Swift):
- Basic unit: Triangle groupings
- Sequence: 1, 3, 5, 7, 9... (odd numbers)
- Pattern rule: +2 triangles each row
- Symbolism: Progress, achievement, pursuit of goals

**Kowhaiwhai Pattern Frequencies**:
- **Mangopare** (Hammerhead shark): 2, 4, 6, 8... (even number sequence)
- **Koru sequences**: 1, 2, 3, 5, 8... (Fibonacci)
- **Rauru** (Peace): 4, 8, 12, 16... (multiples of 4)

### 🌊 **Tidal Patterns - Harbours of Aotearoa**

**Auckland Harbour Tidal Mathematics**
- **High tide pattern**: Every 12 hours 25 minutes
- **Spring tide cycle**: Every 14.7 days
- **Tidal height sequence**: 0.8m, 2.1m, 3.7m, 2.8m, 1.2m, 0.3m
- **Daily pattern**: Rises 50 minutes later each day

**Wellington Harbour Wind Patterns**:
- **Northerly wind days**: 2, 5, 7, 12, 19... (adding previous two numbers)
- **Average wind speed sequence**: 15, 22, 18, 25, 21, 28... (alternating +7, -4)

## Three Ways to Use This Resource

### 🎨 **Activity 1: Creating Koru Spiral Patterns**
**Time**: 25 minutes | **Materials**: Compass, ruler, graph paper

**Task**: Create mathematical koru spirals using the golden ratio
1. **Starting spiral**: Draw circle with 1cm radius
2. **Growing pattern**: Each new spiral = previous × 1.618
3. **Sequence practice**: Calculate next 5 spiral sizes
4. **Cultural connection**: Research koru meanings in your local iwi

**Extension**: Design a school logo using mathematical koru patterns

---

### 🔢 **Activity 2: Tukutuku Pattern Investigation**
**Time**: 30 minutes | **Materials**: Colored pencils, grid paper

**Tasks**:
1. **Pattern recognition**: Complete these tukutuku sequences:
   - Pūhoro triangles: 1, 3, 5, 7, __, __, __
   - Mangopare repeats: 2, 4, 6, __, __, __
   - Rauru groupings: 4, 8, 12, __, __, __

2. **Create your pattern**: Design a new tukutuku pattern using:
   - Rule of your choice (×2, +3, square numbers, etc.)
   - Minimum 6 terms in sequence
   - Cultural meaning explanation

**Cultural Research**: Interview kaumātua or research online about the meaning of traditional patterns in your rohe (region).

---

### 📊 **Activity 3: Natural Pattern Data Analysis**
**Time**: 35 minutes | **Materials**: Calculator, graphing materials

**New Zealand Nature Patterns to Investigate**:

1. **Pōhutukawa Flower Patterns**:
   - Flowers per branch: 5, 12, 17, 29, 46...
   - Find the pattern rule
   - Predict flowers on next branch size

2. **Kōkako Territory Patterns**:
   - Territory sizes (hectares): 3, 6, 12, 24, 48...
   - Identify the relationship
   - Calculate territory for family group of 8

3. **Kakapo Breeding Cycle**:
   - Years between breeding: 2, 4, 6, 3, 5, 7, 4, 6, 8...
   - Find the two alternating patterns
   - Predict next 4 breeding years

**Real Data Challenge**: Research actual data about these native species and compare with the mathematical patterns.

## Assessment Opportunities

### **Formative Assessment Questions**:
- How can you tell if a pattern is growing by addition or multiplication?
- What makes Māori cultural patterns mathematically interesting?
- How do patterns in nature connect to patterns in cultural art?

### **Summative Assessment Task**:
**"Create a Mathematical Taonga"**
Design a piece of cultural art (digital or physical) that:
1. Uses at least two different mathematical patterns
2. Includes authentic New Zealand cultural elements
3. Explains the mathematical rules clearly
4. Shows respect for mātauranga Māori

### **Success Indicators**:
- **Achieved**: Identifies patterns and follows rules correctly
- **Merit**: Explains mathematical relationships and cultural connections  
- **Excellence**: Creates original patterns with sophisticated mathematical reasoning and cultural understanding

## Differentiation Strategies

### **Support for Struggling Learners**:
- Provide pattern number sequences before geometric patterns
- Use physical materials (beans, blocks) before abstract numbers
- Start with simple +1, +2 patterns before ratios

### **Challenge for Advanced Learners**:
- Research actual Fibonacci sequences in NZ native plants
- Create patterns using fractions and decimals
- Design complex composite patterns with multiple rules

### **Cultural Responsiveness**:
- Connect to local iwi patterns and designs
- Invite local artists or kaumātua to share knowledge
- Research family/whānau cultural patterns and stories
