# Statistics and Data Analysis: Understanding Aotearoa - Year 8 Mathematics Unit

## New Zealand Curriculum Integration

**Learning Area**: Mathematics and Statistics  
**Level**: 4 (Year 8)  
**Strand**: Statistics  
**Key Competencies**: Thinking; Using language, symbols and texts; Managing self; Relating to others; Participating and contributing

### Achievement Objectives

- **Level 4**: Students will gain knowledge, skills, and experience to investigate situations by planning and conducting surveys and experiments.

## Unit Overview

This comprehensive unit explores statistics and data analysis through real-world New Zealand contexts. Students will develop understanding of data collection, analysis, and interpretation while engaging with meaningful issues relevant to their lives and communities.

## Learning Intentions

By the end of this unit, students will:

1. Understand the importance of statistics in decision-making
2. Develop skills in data collection and analysis
3. Learn to interpret and communicate statistical information
4. Apply statistical thinking to real-world problems
5. Understand the role of statistics in New Zealand society

## Māori Perspectives on Statistics and Data

### Traditional Māori Data Collection

- **Whakapapa (Genealogy)**: Māori have long collected and analyzed genealogical data to understand relationships and connections
- **Environmental Monitoring**: Traditional knowledge systems include systematic observation and recording of environmental changes
- **Resource Management**: Iwi and hapū have collected data on resource availability and usage for sustainable management

### Contemporary Māori Data

- **Iwi Statistics**: Many iwi collect demographic and social data about their members
- **Health Statistics**: Māori health organizations use data to identify and address health disparities
- **Educational Achievement**: Māori education providers track student outcomes and cultural engagement

### Cultural Considerations in Data Analysis

- **Whanaungatanga (Relationships)**: Understanding that data represents real people and communities
- **Kaitiakitanga (Guardianship)**: Using data responsibly to care for communities and environment
- **Manaakitanga (Hospitality)**: Ensuring data collection and analysis respects and benefits communities

### Real-World Examples

- **Te Kupenga 2013**: New Zealand's Māori social survey
- **Māori Health Statistics**: Health data collection and analysis by Māori health organizations
- **Iwi Development Plans**: Statistical analysis supporting iwi economic and social development

## Lesson 1: Introduction to Statistics and Data

### Learning Intention

Students will understand what statistics are and why they matter in New Zealand society.

### Success Criteria

- Students can define statistics and explain their importance
- Students can identify different types of data
- Students can explain how statistics are used in decision-making

### Activities

#### 1. Statistics in Daily Life (45 minutes)

**Materials**: Newspapers, magazines, websites, digital devices

Students explore how statistics appear in daily life:

- News articles and reports
- Government publications
- Sports statistics
- Weather data
- Social media analytics
- Business and economic reports

**Real New Zealand Examples**:

- Statistics New Zealand data
- Ministry of Education reports
- Health statistics and reports
- Environmental monitoring data
- Census information and analysis

#### 2. Data Types and Sources (40 minutes)

Students investigate different types of data:

- Primary vs. secondary data
- Quantitative vs. qualitative data
- Discrete vs. continuous data
- Categorical vs. numerical data

**Data Source Exploration**:

- Government databases and websites
- Research institutions and universities
- Community organisations and groups
- Media and news sources
- Personal and family data

#### 3. Statistics and Decision-Making (35 minutes)

Students explore how statistics influence decisions:

- Government policy and planning
- Business and economic decisions
- Community and social planning
- Personal and family choices
- Environmental and conservation decisions

### Assessment

- Statistics identification and explanation
- Data type classification accuracy
- Decision-making analysis depth

## Lesson 2: Data Collection and Surveys

### Learning Intention

Students will learn how to collect data effectively through surveys and other methods.

### Success Criteria

- Students can design effective surveys
- Students understand sampling methods
- Students can collect data systematically

### Activities

#### 1. Survey Design Workshop (50 minutes)

Students design surveys on topics relevant to their lives:

- School and education issues
- Community and social concerns
- Environmental and sustainability topics
- Technology and digital media use
- Health and wellbeing

**Survey Design Elements**:

- Clear research questions and objectives
- Appropriate question types and formats
- Sampling strategy and target population
- Data collection methods and tools
- Ethical considerations and consent

#### 2. Sampling Methods Study (40 minutes)

Students explore different sampling approaches:

- Random sampling
- Stratified sampling
- Systematic sampling
- Convenience sampling
- Volunteer sampling

**Real New Zealand Examples**:

- Census sampling methods
- Opinion poll sampling
- Market research sampling
- Health survey sampling
- Educational assessment sampling

#### 3. Data Collection Practice (30 minutes)

Students practice data collection:

- Conducting pilot surveys
- Testing data collection tools
- Refining survey questions
- Training data collectors
- Quality control and validation

### Assessment

- Survey design quality and appropriateness
- Sampling method understanding
- Data collection practice effectiveness

## Lesson 3: Data Analysis and Interpretation

### Learning Intention

Students will learn to analyse and interpret statistical data effectively.

### Success Criteria

- Students can calculate basic statistics
- Students can create appropriate graphs and charts
- Students can interpret statistical results

### Activities

#### 1. Descriptive Statistics (45 minutes)

Students learn to calculate and interpret:

- Measures of central tendency (mean, median, mode)
- Measures of spread (range, standard deviation)
- Percentiles and quartiles
- Frequency distributions
- Summary statistics

**Real New Zealand Data Examples**:

- Student achievement data
- Population demographics
- Economic indicators
- Health statistics
- Environmental measurements

#### 2. Data Visualization (40 minutes)

Students create appropriate graphs and charts:

- Bar charts and histograms
- Line graphs and time series
- Pie charts and proportional displays
- Scatter plots and correlation
- Box plots and distribution displays

**Visualization Principles**:

- Appropriate chart type selection
- Clear labels and titles
- Accurate scaling and proportions
- Color and design considerations
- Accessibility and readability

#### 3. Statistical Interpretation (35 minutes)

Students learn to interpret statistical results:

- Understanding what statistics mean
- Identifying patterns and trends
- Recognizing limitations and uncertainties
- Avoiding common misinterpretations
- Communicating findings clearly

### Assessment

- Statistical calculation accuracy
- Graph and chart quality
- Interpretation depth and insight

## Teacher Notes

### Key Concepts to Emphasise

- Statistics help us understand the world around us
- Data collection and analysis require careful planning
- Statistical results must be interpreted carefully
- Communication of statistical findings is crucial
- Statistics can be used to make informed decisions

### Common Misconceptions to Address

- Statistics are always accurate and reliable
- Correlation implies causation
- Larger samples are always better
- Statistical significance means practical importance
- All data is equally valuable and reliable

### Differentiation Strategies

- Provide multiple entry points for different mathematical abilities
- Use visual, auditory, and kinesthetic approaches
- Offer choice in investigation topics and methods
- Provide additional support for students with learning difficulties
- Challenge advanced students with extension activities

### Cultural Considerations

- Ensure respectful engagement with different cultural perspectives
- Include diverse examples and case studies
- Acknowledge different ways of knowing and understanding
- Create inclusive learning environments
- Respect privacy and confidentiality in data collection

## Cultural Concepts

### Whanaungatanga (Relationships)
Whanaungatanga refers to the traditional Māori concept of relationships and connections between people, places, and things. This concept emphasizes the importance of building and maintaining positive relationships.

### Manaakitanga (Hospitality)
Manaakitanga refers to the traditional Māori concept of hospitality, kindness, and care for others. This concept emphasizes the importance of showing respect and care for all people.

### Kaitiakitanga (Guardianship)
Kaitiakitanga refers to the traditional Māori concept of guardianship and protection of the environment and resources. This concept emphasizes the responsibility to care for and protect the natural world for future generations.

## Conclusion

This comprehensive unit provides students with deep, meaningful understanding of statistics and data analysis through real-world New Zealand contexts. Through authentic engagement with real data and problems, students develop the knowledge, skills, and attitudes needed to be informed, critical consumers and producers of statistical information.

The unit emphasises the importance of statistical thinking in modern society and encourages students to see themselves as capable of understanding and using statistics to make informed decisions about their lives and communities.
