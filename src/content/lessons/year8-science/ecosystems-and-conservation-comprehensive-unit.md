# Ecosystems and Conservation: Aotearoa's Unique Environment - Year 8 Science Unit

## New Zealand Curriculum Integration

**Learning Area**: Science  
**Level**: 4 (Year 8)  
**Strand**: Living World; Nature of Science  
**Key Competencies**: Thinking; Using language, symbols and texts; Managing self; Relating to others; Participating and contributing

### Achievement Objectives

- **Level 4**: Students will gain knowledge, skills, and experience to understand how living things are suited to their particular habitat and how they respond to environmental changes.

## Unit Overview

This comprehensive unit explores New Zealand's unique ecosystems and conservation challenges, integrating Western science with Māori knowledge systems. Students will develop understanding of biodiversity, environmental threats, and conservation strategies through real-world examples and hands-on investigations.

## Learning Intentions

By the end of this unit, students will:

1. Understand New Zealand's unique ecosystems and biodiversity
2. Explore the relationship between Māori knowledge and Western science
3. Investigate environmental threats and their impacts
4. Develop conservation strategies and solutions
5. Apply scientific thinking to real-world environmental issues

## Lesson 1: New Zealand's Unique Ecosystems

### Learning Intention

Students will understand the characteristics and significance of New Zealand's unique ecosystems.

### Success Criteria

- Students can identify key New Zealand ecosystems
- Students understand the concept of endemism
- Students can explain the importance of biodiversity

### Activities

#### 1. Ecosystem Mapping and Research (50 minutes)

**Materials**: Large maps, research materials, digital tools

Students research and map New Zealand's major ecosystems:

- Native forests (podocarp, beech, kauri)
- Alpine and subalpine zones
- Coastal and marine environments
- Wetlands and freshwater systems
- Grasslands and shrublands

**Real New Zealand Examples**:

- Fiordland National Park
- Tongariro National Park
- Waitakere Ranges
- Canterbury Plains
- West Coast rainforests

#### 2. Endemic Species Investigation (40 minutes)

Students research New Zealand's unique species including:

- Flightless birds (kiwi, takahe, kakapo)
- Ancient reptiles (tuatara)
- Unique plants (silver fern, kauri, pohutukawa)
- Marine mammals (Hector's dolphin, New Zealand sea lion)
- Invertebrates (weta, giant land snails)

**Investigation Framework**:

- Physical characteristics and adaptations
- Habitat requirements and distribution
- Ecological roles and relationships
- Conservation status and threats

#### 3. Biodiversity Comparison (30 minutes)

Students compare New Zealand's biodiversity with:

- Other island nations
- Continental ecosystems
- Similar climate zones
- Historical vs. current biodiversity

### Assessment

- Ecosystem map accuracy and detail
- Species investigation depth
- Biodiversity comparison analysis

## Lesson 2: Māori Knowledge and Western Science

### Learning Intention

Students will explore the relationship between Māori knowledge systems and Western scientific understanding.

### Success Criteria

- Students can identify key concepts in Māori environmental knowledge
- Students understand the value of traditional knowledge
- Students can compare different knowledge systems

### Activities

#### 1. Kaitiakitanga Exploration (45 minutes)

Students investigate the concept of kaitiakitanga including:

- Definition and cultural significance
- Traditional practices and protocols
- Modern applications and relevance
- Examples from different iwi

**Real New Zealand Examples**:

- Tūhoe's sustainable development initiatives
- Whanganui River legal personhood
- Te Urewera management
- Local iwi conservation projects

#### 2. Traditional Ecological Knowledge Study (40 minutes)

Students explore traditional knowledge about:

- Seasonal cycles and environmental indicators
- Plant and animal relationships
- Sustainable resource management
- Environmental monitoring and observation

#### 3. Knowledge Integration Workshop (35 minutes)

Students examine how traditional and scientific knowledge can work together:

- Case studies of successful integration
- Challenges and opportunities
- Respectful collaboration principles
- Future possibilities and applications

### Assessment

- Kaitiakitanga understanding and examples
- Traditional knowledge analysis
- Integration workshop participation

## Lesson 3: Environmental Threats and Impacts

### Learning Intention

Students will investigate the major threats to New Zealand's ecosystems and their impacts.

### Success Criteria

- Students can identify key environmental threats
- Students understand the mechanisms of impact
- Students can analyse cause and effect relationships

### Activities

#### 1. Threat Analysis Investigation (50 minutes)

Students research major threats including:

- Introduced species (possums, stoats, rats, deer)
- Habitat destruction and fragmentation
- Climate change impacts
- Pollution and contamination
- Human activities and development

**Investigation Method**:

- Threat identification and description
- Impact mechanisms and processes
- Affected species and ecosystems
- Historical and current trends
- Future projections and scenarios

#### 2. Case Study Analysis (40 minutes)

Students analyse specific case studies:

- Kakapo recovery programme
- Kauri dieback disease
- Marine pollution incidents
- Urban development impacts
- Climate change effects on alpine ecosystems

#### 3. Impact Assessment (30 minutes)

Students assess the severity and urgency of different threats:

- Ecological impact scale
- Economic and social costs
- Reversibility and recovery potential
- Priority for action and intervention

### Assessment

- Threat analysis depth and accuracy
- Case study analysis quality
- Impact assessment reasoning

## Lesson 4: Conservation Strategies and Solutions

### Learning Intention

Students will explore various conservation strategies and their effectiveness.

### Success Criteria

- Students can identify different conservation approaches
- Students understand the principles behind strategies
- Students can evaluate effectiveness and limitations

### Activities

#### 1. Conservation Strategy Research (45 minutes)

Students investigate different approaches including:

- Protected areas and reserves
- Species recovery programmes
- Habitat restoration and enhancement
- Community-based conservation
- Scientific research and monitoring

**Real New Zealand Examples**:

- Department of Conservation programmes
- Community conservation groups
- Iwi-led conservation initiatives
- University research projects
- Corporate conservation partnerships

#### 2. Success Story Analysis (40 minutes)

Students analyse successful conservation projects:

- Takahe recovery programme
- Kiwi protection initiatives
- Marine reserve establishment
- Forest restoration projects
- Community-led conservation efforts

#### 3. Strategy Evaluation (35 minutes)

Students evaluate different strategies based on:

- Effectiveness and success rates
- Cost and resource requirements
- Community support and involvement
- Long-term sustainability
- Potential for replication

### Assessment

- Conservation strategy research depth
- Success story analysis quality
- Strategy evaluation reasoning

## Lesson 5: Action and Application

### Learning Intention

Students will develop and implement conservation action plans.

### Success Criteria

- Students can create feasible action plans
- Students can apply scientific thinking to real problems
- Students can work collaboratively on solutions

### Activities

#### 1. Local Conservation Assessment (40 minutes)

Students assess their local environment:

- Biodiversity inventory
- Threat identification
- Conservation opportunities
- Community resources and support
- Potential partnerships and collaborations

#### 2. Action Plan Development (45 minutes)

Students create conservation action plans including:

- Problem identification and analysis
- Goal setting and objectives
- Strategy selection and justification
- Implementation timeline and steps
- Monitoring and evaluation methods

#### 3. Community Engagement (35 minutes)

Students plan community engagement including:

- Education and awareness raising
- Volunteer recruitment and training
- Fundraising and resource gathering
- Partnership development
- Celebration and recognition

### Assessment

- Local assessment accuracy and insight
- Action plan feasibility and creativity
- Community engagement planning

## Assessment Rubric

### Excellent (4)

- Demonstrates deep understanding of New Zealand's ecosystems and conservation
- Shows sophisticated analysis of environmental threats and solutions
- Creates innovative and feasible conservation action plans
- Makes meaningful connections between traditional and scientific knowledge
- Engages effectively with community and stakeholders

### Good (3)

- Shows solid understanding of ecosystems and conservation principles
- Analyses environmental issues with good insight
- Creates reasonable conservation action plans
- Makes some connections between different knowledge systems
- Engages appropriately with community

### Satisfactory (2)

- Shows basic understanding of ecosystems and conservation
- Identifies environmental issues with some analysis
- Creates simple conservation action plans
- Makes limited connections between knowledge systems
- Engages minimally with community

### Needs Support (1)

- Shows limited understanding of ecosystems and conservation
- Struggles to identify and analyse environmental issues
- Creates minimal conservation action plans
- Makes few connections between knowledge systems
- Engages little with community

## Teacher Notes

### Key Concepts to Emphasise

- New Zealand's ecosystems are unique and globally significant
- Traditional and scientific knowledge can work together
- Environmental threats require urgent and coordinated action
- Conservation success depends on community involvement
- Individual actions can make a difference

### Common Misconceptions to Address

- Conservation is only about protecting individual species
- Traditional knowledge is not scientific
- Environmental problems are too big for individuals to address
- New Zealand's environment is not under threat
- Conservation and development are incompatible

### Differentiation Strategies

- Provide multiple entry points for different learning styles
- Use visual, auditory, and kinesthetic approaches
- Offer choice in investigation methods and assessment
- Provide additional support for English language learners
- Challenge advanced students with extension activities

### Cultural Considerations

- Ensure respectful engagement with Māori knowledge and culture
- Invite Māori community members to share their perspectives
- Use appropriate protocols and tikanga
- Acknowledge different cultural perspectives and values
- Create safe spaces for all students to participate

## Conclusion

This comprehensive unit provides students with deep, meaningful understanding of New Zealand's unique ecosystems and conservation challenges. Through authentic engagement with real-world issues and multiple knowledge systems, students develop the knowledge, skills, and attitudes needed to be informed, active environmental citizens.

The unit emphasises the importance of both traditional and scientific knowledge in addressing environmental challenges and encourages students to see themselves as part of New Zealand's conservation story.
