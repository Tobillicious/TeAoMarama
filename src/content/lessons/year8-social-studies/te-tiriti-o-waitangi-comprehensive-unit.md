# Te Tiriti o Waitangi: A Comprehensive Year 8 Social Studies Unit

## New Zealand Curriculum Integration

**Learning Area**: Social Sciences  
**Level**: 4 (Year 8)  
**Strand**: Identity, Culture, and Organisation  
**Key Competencies**: Thinking, Using language, symbols and texts, Managing self, Relating to others, Participating and contributing

### Achievement Objectives

- **Level 4**: Students will gain knowledge, skills, and experience to understand how cultural practices vary but reflect similar purposes.

## Unit Overview

This comprehensive unit explores Te Tiriti o Waitangi as a living document that continues to shape New Zealand society. Students will develop deep understanding through multiple perspectives, real-world examples, and cross-curricular connections.

## Learning Intentions

By the end of this unit, students will:

1. Understand the historical context and significance of Te Tiriti o Waitangi
2. Analyse different perspectives on the Treaty and its implications
3. Explore contemporary issues related to the Treaty
4. Develop critical thinking skills through source analysis
5. Create meaningful connections between past and present

## Lesson 1: Historical Context and Background

### Learning Intention

Students will understand the historical context that led to the signing of Te Tiriti o Waitangi.

### Success Criteria

- Students can explain the key events leading to 1840
- Students understand the different motivations of Māori and British
- Students can identify key historical figures and their roles

### Activities

#### 1. Timeline Creation (45 minutes)

**Materials**: Large paper, markers, historical images, digital timeline tool

Students create a comprehensive timeline from 1769-1840 including:

- Captain Cook's arrival (1769)
- Early European contact and trade
- Musket Wars (1807-1842)
- Missionary arrival and influence
- British government interest in New Zealand
- The signing of Te Tiriti o Waitangi (1840)

**Real New Zealand Example**: Visit to Te Papa's Treaty exhibition or virtual tour

#### 2. Source Analysis: Different Perspectives (30 minutes)

**Primary Sources**:

- Captain Cook's journal entries about Māori
- Missionary letters describing Māori society
- British government documents about New Zealand
- Māori oral histories and traditions

Students analyse these sources to understand different worldviews and motivations.

#### 3. Role Play: The Lead-up to 1840 (30 minutes)

Students take on roles of:

- Māori rangatira (chiefs)
- British officials
- Missionaries
- Traders
- Whalers

They debate the pros and cons of British involvement in New Zealand.

### Assessment

- Timeline accuracy and detail
- Source analysis depth
- Role play participation and understanding

## Lesson 2: The Treaty Documents and Translation Issues

### Learning Intention

Students will understand the content of Te Tiriti o Waitangi and the significance of translation differences.

### Success Criteria

- Students can identify the key articles of the Treaty
- Students understand the differences between Māori and English versions
- Students can explain the implications of these differences

### Activities

#### 1. Treaty Text Analysis (40 minutes)

Students compare the English and Māori versions of the Treaty, focusing on:

- Article 1: Sovereignty vs Kawanatanga
- Article 2: Property rights vs Tino rangatiratanga
- Article 3: Citizenship and protection

**Real New Zealand Example**: Analysis of actual Treaty documents at Archives New Zealand

#### 2. Translation Workshop (35 minutes)

Students work with Te Reo Māori speakers to understand:

- The concept of tino rangatiratanga
- The meaning of kawanatanga
- Cultural concepts that don't translate directly

#### 3. Modern Implications Discussion (30 minutes)

Students discuss how these translation differences affect:

- Land ownership
- Resource management
- Political representation
- Cultural rights

### Assessment

- Translation analysis accuracy
- Understanding of cultural concepts
- Discussion participation

## Lesson 3: Contemporary Treaty Issues

### Learning Intention

Students will explore how Te Tiriti o Waitangi continues to influence New Zealand society today.

### Success Criteria

- Students can identify current Treaty-related issues
- Students understand different perspectives on these issues
- Students can form and justify their own opinions

### Activities

#### 1. Current Events Research (45 minutes)

Students research recent Treaty-related events:

- Waitangi Tribunal decisions
- Resource management cases
- Political debates
- Cultural celebrations and protests

**Real New Zealand Examples**:

- Ihumātao land occupation
- Whanganui River legal personhood
- Te Urewera management
- Auckland Council Treaty partnerships

#### 2. Stakeholder Analysis (40 minutes)

For each issue, students identify and analyse different stakeholder perspectives:

- Māori iwi and hapū
- Government agencies
- Local councils
- Business interests
- Environmental groups
- General public

#### 3. Debate: Treaty Principles in Practice (35 minutes)

Students debate current issues such as:

- Should the Treaty be taught in all schools?
- How should Treaty settlements be managed?
- What role should Māori have in resource management?

### Assessment

- Research depth and accuracy
- Stakeholder analysis quality
- Debate participation and argumentation

## Lesson 4: Cross-Curricular Connections

### Learning Intention

Students will explore connections between Te Tiriti o Waitangi and other learning areas.

### Success Criteria

- Students can identify connections to other subjects
- Students understand interdisciplinary approaches
- Students can apply learning across contexts

### Activities

#### 1. Science Connection: Environmental Management (40 minutes)

Students explore how Treaty principles apply to:

- Climate change adaptation
- Biodiversity protection
- Water quality management
- Renewable energy development

**Real New Zealand Example**: Tūhoe's sustainable development initiatives

#### 2. Mathematics Connection: Statistical Analysis (35 minutes)

Students analyse data on:

- Treaty settlement amounts over time
- Māori representation in various sectors
- Educational achievement gaps
- Health and social statistics

#### 3. Arts Connection: Creative Expression (45 minutes)

Students create artworks that express their understanding of:

- Treaty principles
- Cultural identity
- Historical events
- Contemporary issues

**Real New Zealand Example**: Study of contemporary Māori artists like Lisa Reihana

### Assessment

- Cross-curricular connection identification
- Data analysis accuracy
- Creative expression quality

## Lesson 5: Action and Reflection

### Learning Intention

Students will develop action plans for promoting Treaty understanding and reflect on their learning.

### Success Criteria

- Students can create meaningful action plans
- Students can reflect critically on their learning
- Students can identify future learning needs

### Activities

#### 1. Action Planning (40 minutes)

Students develop plans to:

- Promote Treaty understanding in their community
- Support Māori cultural events
- Learn more about local iwi history
- Advocate for Treaty education

#### 2. Community Engagement (35 minutes)

Students research and plan:

- Visits to local marae
- Interviews with community leaders
- Participation in cultural events
- School-based Treaty education initiatives

#### 3. Reflection and Future Learning (30 minutes)

Students reflect on:

- What they've learned
- How their understanding has changed
- What questions they still have
- How they can continue learning

### Assessment

- Action plan feasibility and creativity
- Community engagement planning
- Reflection depth and honesty

## Assessment Rubric

### Excellent (4)

- Demonstrates deep understanding of Treaty history and contemporary relevance
- Shows sophisticated analysis of different perspectives
- Makes meaningful connections across learning areas
- Creates innovative and feasible action plans
- Reflects critically on learning and identifies future needs

### Good (3)

- Shows solid understanding of Treaty content and context
- Analyses different perspectives with some depth
- Makes some cross-curricular connections
- Creates reasonable action plans
- Reflects on learning with some insight

### Satisfactory (2)

- Shows basic understanding of Treaty content
- Identifies different perspectives
- Makes limited cross-curricular connections
- Creates simple action plans
- Provides basic reflection on learning

### Needs Support (1)

- Shows limited understanding of Treaty content
- Struggles to identify different perspectives
- Makes few cross-curricular connections
- Creates minimal action plans
- Provides limited reflection on learning

## Extension Activities

- Research specific Treaty claims and settlements
- Analyse international comparisons (Canada, Australia, USA)

- Explore Treaty implications for new technologies

Students research their local area's Treaty history and Māori connections.

### Week 3: Current Events

Students find and summarise recent news articles about Treaty-related issues.

### Week 4: Creative Project

Students create a family presentation about what they've learned.

## Teacher Notes

### Key Concepts to Emphasise

- Te Tiriti o Waitangi is a living document
- Different perspectives are valid and important
- Historical context shapes contemporary understanding
- Treaty principles apply across all areas of life
- Learning is ongoing and collaborative

### Common Misconceptions to Address

- The Treaty is only about the past
- There is one "correct" interpretation
- Treaty issues only affect Māori
- The Treaty is not relevant to young people
- Treaty education is only for Māori students

### Differentiation Strategies

- Provide multiple entry points for different learning styles
- Use visual, auditory, and kinesthetic approaches
- Offer choice in assessment methods
- Provide additional support for English language learners
- Challenge advanced students with extension activities

### Cultural Considerations

- Ensure respectful engagement with Māori culture
- Invite Māori community members to share their perspectives
- Use appropriate protocols and tikanga
- Acknowledge different cultural perspectives
- Create safe spaces for all students to participate

## Conclusion

This comprehensive unit provides students with deep, meaningful learning about Te Tiriti o Waitangi that connects past and present, theory and practice, and different learning areas. Through authentic engagement with real-world issues and multiple perspectives, students develop the knowledge, skills, and attitudes needed to be informed, active citizens in Aotearoa New Zealand.

The unit emphasises the ongoing relevance of the Treaty and encourages students to see themselves as part of New Zealand's continuing story of partnership and understanding.
