# Māori Literature: Voices of Aotearoa - Year 8 English Unit

## New Zealand Curriculum Integration

**Learning Area**: English  
**Level**: 4 (Year 8)  
**Strand**: Listening, Reading, and Viewing; Speaking, Writing, and Presenting  
**Key Competencies**: Using language, symbols and texts; Thinking; Relating to others; Managing self; Participating and contributing

### Achievement Objectives

- **Level 4**: Students will gain knowledge, skills, and experience to understand how language features are used for effect within and across texts.

## Unit Overview

This comprehensive unit explores Māori literature as a vital part of New Zealand's literary landscape. Students will engage with traditional and contemporary Māori texts, developing understanding of cultural perspectives, literary techniques, and the power of storytelling.

## Learning Intentions

By the end of this unit, students will:

1. Understand the significance of Māori literature in New Zealand culture
2. Analyse literary techniques used in Māori texts
3. Explore themes of identity, culture, and belonging
4. Develop skills in critical reading and analysis
5. Create their own texts inspired by Māori literary traditions

## Lesson 1: Introduction to Māori Literature

### Learning Intention

Students will understand the scope and significance of Māori literature in New Zealand.

### Success Criteria

- Students can identify different types of Māori literature
- Students understand the cultural context of Māori storytelling
- Students can explain the importance of Māori voices in literature

### Activities

#### 1. Literature Mapping (45 minutes)

**Materials**: Chart paper, markers, digital tools

Students create a comprehensive map of Māori literature including:

- Traditional oral stories and legends
- Contemporary novels and short stories
- Poetry and song lyrics
- Children's literature
- Non-fiction and memoirs
- Digital and multimedia texts

**Real New Zealand Examples**:

- Witi Ihimaera's "The Whale Rider"
- Patricia Grace's "Potiki"
- Hone Tuwhare's poetry
- Keri Hulme's "The Bone People"
- Contemporary authors like Becky Manawatu and Whiti Hereaka

#### 2. Cultural Context Exploration (40 minutes)

Students research and present on:

- The role of storytelling in Māori culture
- Traditional narrative structures
- The importance of oral tradition
- Contemporary Māori identity and expression

#### 3. Text Selection and Justification (35 minutes)

Students select one Māori text to study in depth and justify their choice based on:

- Personal interest
- Literary merit
- Cultural significance
- Accessibility and appropriateness

### Assessment

- Literature map completeness and accuracy
- Cultural context understanding
- Text selection justification

## Lesson 2: Traditional Stories and Legends

### Learning Intention

Students will explore traditional Māori stories and understand their literary and cultural significance.

### Success Criteria

- Students can identify key themes in traditional stories
- Students understand narrative structures and techniques
- Students can analyse cultural values and beliefs

### Activities

#### 1. Story Analysis Workshop (50 minutes)

Students analyse traditional stories including:

- "Maui and the Sun" - themes of determination and innovation
- "Rangi and Papa" - creation myths and family relationships
- "Kupe and the Giant Octopus" - exploration and discovery
- "Tāne Mahuta" - environmental stewardship and respect

**Analysis Framework**:

- Plot structure and narrative techniques
- Character development and motivation
- Themes and messages
- Cultural values and beliefs
- Language features and imagery

#### 2. Oral Storytelling Practice (40 minutes)

Students practice telling traditional stories using:

- Appropriate voice and tone
- Gestures and body language
- Audience engagement techniques
- Cultural protocols and respect

#### 3. Modern Retellings (30 minutes)

Students explore contemporary retellings of traditional stories:

- Picture books and children's versions
- Modern adaptations and interpretations
- Digital and multimedia presentations
- Cross-cultural comparisons

### Assessment

- Story analysis depth and insight
- Oral storytelling skills
- Understanding of cultural significance

## Lesson 3: Contemporary Māori Literature

### Learning Intention

Students will engage with contemporary Māori literature and understand its relevance to modern New Zealand.

### Success Criteria

- Students can identify contemporary themes and issues
- Students understand different literary techniques
- Students can connect texts to their own experiences

### Activities

#### 1. Novel Study: "The Whale Rider" (60 minutes)

**Pre-reading Activities**:

- Research on Witi Ihimaera and his background
- Exploration of the film adaptation
- Discussion of themes and expectations

**Reading and Analysis**:

- Character development and relationships
- Themes of tradition vs. change
- Cultural identity and belonging
- Gender roles and expectations
- Environmental themes and connections

**Post-reading Activities**:

- Comparison with film adaptation
- Discussion of contemporary relevance
- Personal response and reflection

#### 2. Poetry Analysis (40 minutes)

Students analyse poems by Māori poets including:

- Hone Tuwhare's "Rain"
- Apirana Taylor's "Sad Joke on a Marae"
- Hinemoana Baker's contemporary works
- Student-selected poems

**Analysis Focus**:

- Imagery and symbolism
- Language techniques and effects
- Themes and messages
- Cultural and personal perspectives
- Emotional impact and response

#### 3. Short Story Collection (50 minutes)

Students read and analyse short stories from:

- Patricia Grace's collections
- Contemporary Māori short story anthologies
- Student-selected stories

**Analysis Framework**:

- Narrative techniques and structure
- Character development and motivation
- Themes and cultural perspectives
- Language use and style
- Personal connections and responses

### Assessment

- Novel study analysis and insight
- Poetry analysis depth and understanding
- Short story analysis and personal response

## Lesson 4: Themes and Literary Techniques

### Learning Intention

Students will identify and analyse key themes and literary techniques in Māori literature.

### Success Criteria

- Students can identify recurring themes
- Students understand various literary techniques
- Students can analyse the effectiveness of techniques

### Activities

#### 1. Theme Analysis Workshop (45 minutes)

Students identify and analyse key themes including:

- Identity and belonging
- Family and community
- Tradition and change
- Land and environment
- Cultural pride and resilience
- Colonisation and its effects
- Healing and reconciliation

**Analysis Method**:

- Text evidence and examples
- Author's purpose and message
- Reader response and impact
- Cultural and historical context

#### 2. Literary Techniques Study (40 minutes)

Students explore techniques including:

- Symbolism and metaphor
- Imagery and sensory language
- Dialogue and voice
- Narrative perspective
- Structure and pacing
- Cultural references and allusions

#### 3. Comparative Analysis (35 minutes)

Students compare:

- Traditional vs. contemporary approaches
- Different authors' styles and techniques
- Themes across different texts
- Cultural perspectives and values

### Assessment

- Theme analysis depth and insight
- Literary technique identification and analysis
- Comparative analysis quality

## Lesson 5: Creative Response and Personal Expression

### Learning Intention

Students will create their own texts inspired by Māori literary traditions and themes.

### Success Criteria

- Students can create original texts
- Students can use appropriate literary techniques
- Students can express personal and cultural perspectives

### Activities

#### 1. Creative Writing Workshop (50 minutes)

Students create original texts including:

- Short stories inspired by Māori themes
- Poetry expressing personal identity
- Personal narratives about belonging
- Cultural reflections and observations

**Writing Process**:

- Planning and brainstorming
- Drafting and revision
- Peer feedback and editing
- Final presentation and sharing

#### 2. Digital Storytelling (40 minutes)

Students create multimedia presentations including:

- Digital stories with images and sound
- Video presentations of their writing
- Podcast-style audio recordings
- Interactive digital texts

#### 3. Performance and Presentation (30 minutes)

Students present their work through:

- Oral readings and performances
- Visual presentations and displays
- Group performances and collaborations
- Community sharing and celebration

### Assessment

- Creative writing quality and originality
- Digital storytelling effectiveness
- Performance and presentation skills

## Assessment Rubric

### Excellent (4)

- Demonstrates deep understanding of Māori literature and cultural significance
- Shows sophisticated analysis of themes and techniques
- Creates original, engaging texts with appropriate techniques
- Makes meaningful connections between texts and personal experience
- Presents work effectively and engages audience

### Good (3)

- Shows solid understanding of Māori literature and themes
- Analyses texts with good insight and evidence
- Creates original texts with some effective techniques
- Makes some connections between texts and experience
- Presents work clearly and engages audience

### Satisfactory (2)

- Shows basic understanding of Māori literature
- Identifies themes and techniques with some analysis
- Creates simple original texts
- Makes limited connections between texts and experience
- Presents work adequately

### Needs Support (1)

- Shows limited understanding of Māori literature
- Struggles to identify themes and techniques
- Creates minimal original texts
- Makes few connections between texts and experience
- Presents work with difficulty

## Teacher Notes

### Key Concepts to Emphasise

- Māori literature is a vital part of New Zealand's literary heritage
- Traditional and contemporary texts offer different perspectives
- Themes of identity, culture, and belonging are universal
- Literary techniques enhance meaning and impact
- Personal response and cultural understanding are both important

### Common Misconceptions to Address

- Māori literature is only for Māori students
- Traditional stories are just children's tales
- Contemporary Māori literature is not "real" literature
- Cultural context is not important for understanding
- All Māori literature is the same

### Differentiation Strategies

- Provide multiple entry points for different reading levels
- Use visual, auditory, and kinesthetic approaches
- Offer choice in text selection and assessment methods
- Provide additional support for English language learners
- Challenge advanced students with extension activities

### Cultural Considerations

- Ensure respectful engagement with Māori culture and literature
- Invite Māori community members to share their perspectives
- Use appropriate protocols and tikanga
- Acknowledge different cultural perspectives and experiences
- Create safe spaces for all students to participate and share

## Conclusion

This comprehensive unit provides students with deep, meaningful engagement with Māori literature that develops both literary understanding and cultural awareness. Through authentic engagement with traditional and contemporary texts, students develop the knowledge, skills, and attitudes needed to appreciate and contribute to New Zealand's rich literary heritage.

The unit emphasises the ongoing relevance of Māori literature and encourages students to see themselves as part of New Zealand's continuing story of cultural expression and understanding.
