# Y7-9 Maths Games Collection

*Engaging Activities for Numeracy Development in Aotearoa*

## Learning Intention

**WALT**: We are learning to strengthen our numeracy skills through interactive games and challenges.
**Success Criteria**:

- I can apply my knowledge of number, algebra, and geometry in a game context.
- I can use strategic thinking to solve mathematical problems.
- I can collaborate with peers to explore mathematical concepts.
- I can see connections between mathematics and New Zealand culture.

---

## Game 1: Pāmua, Pāmuri (Before, After) - Number Line Challenge

### Mathematical Focus

- **Strand**: Number
- **Skills**: Ordering decimals, fractions, and integers; understanding number properties.

### How to Play

1. **Setup**: Create a large number line on the floor with masking tape, with ‘0’ in the middle and ‘1’ and ‘-1’ at either end.
2. **Gameplay**:
    - The teacher calls out a number (e.g., 0.75, -1/2, 0.33).
    - A student has to stand on the number line where they estimate that number should be.
    - The next student is given a new number and must place themselves correctly in relation to the students already on the line.
    - If a student thinks someone is in the wrong place, they can challenge it. The class discusses and checks with calculators to resolve.
3. **Winning**: The goal is collaborative accuracy, not winning or losing.

### Cultural Connection: Whakapapa

- **Concept**: This game mirrors the concept of **whakapapa** (genealogy), where everything has a specific place and relationship to everything else. Just as people are placed in a family tree, numbers are placed on a number line.
- **Language**: Use Te Reo Māori for numbers to build fluency.

## Game 2: Manu Tukutuku Kites - Geometry Challenge

### Mathematical Focus

- **Strand**: Geometry and Measurement
- **Skills**: Identifying shapes, calculating area and perimeter, understanding symmetry.

### How to Play

1. **Setup**: Provide students with paper, rulers, protractors, and scissors.
2. **Gameplay**:
    - In small groups, students design a **manu tukutuku** (traditional Māori kite).
    - The design must be symmetrical and include at least four different geometric shapes (e.g., rhombus, triangle, trapezium).
    - Groups must calculate the area and perimeter of each shape and the total area of their kite.
    - **Challenge**: Create a kite with the largest area using a single A4 sheet of paper.
3. **Sharing**: Groups present their kites, explaining the geometric properties and calculations.

### Cultural Connection: Matariki

- **Concept**: Manu tukutuku are traditionally flown during **Matariki** (the Māori New Year). This game connects geometric concepts to a significant cultural celebration. Discuss the cultural importance of kite flying in connecting heaven and earth.

## Game 3: Ki-o-Rahi Maths - Algebraic Thinking

### Mathematical Focus

- **Strand**: Algebra
- **Skills**: Using variables, solving simple linear equations, strategic thinking.

### How to Play

1. **Setup**: Based on the traditional Māori game **Ki-o-Rahi**. Set up a simplified field. Designate two teams: *Kīoma* (attackers) and *Taniwha* (defenders).
2. **Gameplay with a Maths Twist**:
    - The *Kīoma* team scores points by touching the *tupu* (central marker) and running to the outer boundary. Let 'k' be the number of successful runs.
    - The *Taniwha* team scores by hitting the *tupu* with the *kī* (ball). Let 't' be the number of hits.
    - The final score is calculated using a formula, e.g., **Score = (5k + 10) - 7t**.
    - After each round, students must calculate their team's score.
3. **Winning**: The team with the highest score after a set number of rounds wins.

### Cultural Connection: Traditional Māori Games

- **Concept**: This activity immerses students in a traditional Māori game while embedding algebraic concepts. It promotes physical activity (**tinana**), strategic thinking (**hinengaro**), and teamwork (**whanaungatanga**).

## Teacher Notes

### **Preparation**

- **Cultural Sensitivity**: If you are not Māori, consult with local iwi or Māori colleagues to ensure cultural concepts like Ki-o-Rahi and manu tukutuku are taught respectfully and accurately.
- **Resources**: Prepare number cards for Game 1, have geometry supplies for Game 2, and set up a safe playing field for Game 3.

### **Differentiation**

- **Support**:
  - **Game 1**: Use only integers or simple fractions.
  - **Game 2**: Provide shape templates.
  - **Game 3**: Use a simpler scoring formula (e.g., Score = 2k - t).
- **Extension**:
  - **Game 1**: Include irrational numbers like π or √2.
  - **Game 2**: Require compound shapes or calculation of surface area for 3D kites.
  - **Game 3**: Have students design and justify their own scoring formula.

### **Assessment Opportunities**

- **Formative**: Observe students' strategies, discussions, and calculations during the games. Use a checklist to note skills demonstrated.
- **Self-Assessment**: Ask students to reflect on which game challenged them the most and what mathematical skills they used.

### **Cross-Curricular Connections**

- **Physical Education**: Game 3 is a full physical activity.
- **Art & Design**: Game 2 involves creative design.
- **Social Studies**: Discuss the history and cultural significance of the games and concepts.
