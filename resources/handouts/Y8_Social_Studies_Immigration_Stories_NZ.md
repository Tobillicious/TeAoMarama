# Immigration Stories of Aotearoa - Year 8 Social Studies

*Understanding how diverse peoples have shaped modern New Zealand*

## Learning Intention

**WALT**: We are learning to understand how immigration has shaped New Zealand society and identity
**Success Criteria**:

- I can identify major waves of immigration to New Zealand
- I can analyze the experiences of different immigrant groups
- I can evaluate the impact of immigration on New Zealand culture
- I can connect past immigration patterns to contemporary New Zealand

## Rich Text Resource: Voices from Many Shores

### 🚢 **The Great Migrations to Aotearoa**

**Wave 1: Polynesian Voyagers (1000-1300 CE)**

- **Origins**: Eastern Polynesia (likely Hawaiki)
- **Vessels**: Waka hourua (double-hulled voyaging canoes)
- **Navigation**: Stars, currents, bird patterns, wave formations
- **Settlement pattern**: Coastal areas first, then inland expansion
- **Cultural impact**: Established Māori society, place names, ecological management

**Wave 2: European Settlement (1840s-1920s)**

- **Peak period**: 1870s-1880s (gold rush era)
- **Main origins**: Britain (85%), Ireland (12%), other Europe (3%)
- **Government schemes**: Assisted passages, land grants
- **Settlement data**: 250,000 arrivals between 1840-1880
- **Regional focus**: Canterbury, Otago, Auckland, Wellington

**Wave 3: Pacific Peoples Migration (1950s-1980s)**

- **Labor recruitment**: Post-WWII labor shortages
- **Main origins**: Samoa, Cook Islands, Tonga, Fiji, Niue
- **Settlement patterns**: Auckland (65%), Wellington (25%)
- **Community development**: Churches, cultural groups, extended family networks
- **Modern status**: 381,642 Pacific peoples (2023 Census - 8.1% of population)

**Wave 4: Asian Immigration (1990s-Present)**

- **Policy changes**: 1991 Immigration Act, points system
- **Main origins**: China (30%), India (25%), Philippines (15%), Korea (10%)
- **Settlement preferences**: Auckland (75%), Wellington (15%)
- **Contributions**: Business, education, technology, cultural diversity
- **Current status**: 861,573 Asian New Zealanders (2023 Census - 15.8%)

### 📊 **Immigration Data Analysis (2023)**

**New Zealand Born vs. Overseas Born**:

- **New Zealand born**: 74.2% of population (3.6 million)
- **Overseas born**: 25.8% of population (1.25 million)
- **Recent arrivals** (2018-2023): 312,000 people
- **Languages spoken**: 164 languages other than English

**Top 10 Countries of Birth (Overseas-born residents)**:

1. **China**: 95,394 people
2. **India**: 85,416 people  
3. **United Kingdom**: 82,314 people
4. **Samoa**: 67,689 people
5. **Philippines**: 63,762 people
6. **Fiji**: 46,953 people
7. **South Africa**: 44,127 people
8. **Australia**: 35,826 people
9. **Tonga**: 34,578 people
10. **Cook Islands**: 28,965 people

### 🎯 **Regional Settlement Patterns**

**Auckland Region** (Immigration hub - 1.7 million total):

- **Overseas-born**: 42% of Auckland population
- **Major communities**: Chinese (8.2%), Indian (7.3%), Pacific (15.4%)
- **Languages**: Mandarin, Hindi, Samoan most common after English
- **Settlement suburbs**:
  - Chinese: East Auckland, North Shore
  - Indian: South Auckland, West Auckland
  - Pacific: South Auckland, West Auckland

**Canterbury Region** (Post-earthquake recovery):

- **Overseas-born**: 22% of Canterbury population
- **Recent growth**: +15% immigrant population since 2011
- **Labor sectors**: Agriculture, construction, education, healthcare
- **Community features**: Multicultural festivals, ethnic restaurants, cultural centers

## Three Ways to Use This Resource

### 🗺️ **Activity 1: Migration Mapping Project**

**Time**: 45 minutes | **Materials**: World map, colored pencils, population data

**Research Task**: Create a visual representation of New Zealand's immigration story

1. **Historical timeline**: Map the four major migration waves with different colors
2. **Modern analysis**: Plot top 10 countries of birth on world map
3. **Regional distribution**: Show where different communities settle in NZ
4. **Personal connection**: Research and add your own family's migration story

**Questions to investigate**:

- Why did people leave their home countries? (Push factors)
- What attracted them to New Zealand? (Pull factors)
- How did government policies affect immigration patterns?
- What challenges did new arrivals face?

**Extension**: Interview a recent immigrant or long-time New Zealand family about their settlement experience.

---

### 📚 **Activity 2: Immigration Story Analysis**

**Time**: 40 minutes | **Materials**: Historical accounts, census data, local resources

**Comparative Study**: Examine different immigrant experiences across time periods

**Case Study 1 - European Settlement (1870s)**:
*Maria and Giuseppe Benedetti - Italian immigrants*

- **Departure**: Left poverty in Northern Italy, 1876
- **Journey**: 8-week voyage via steamship to Wellington
- **Settlement**: Market gardening in Hutt Valley
- **Challenges**: Language barriers, climate adjustment, community acceptance
- **Success factors**: Hard work, Italian community support, agricultural skills

**Case Study 2 - Pacific Migration (1960s)**:
*Sione and Mele Vuki - Tongan immigrants*

- **Departure**: Seeking employment opportunities, 1968
- **Journey**: Flight to Auckland via Fiji
- **Settlement**: Factory work in South Auckland
- **Challenges**: Housing shortages, cultural adjustment, Dawn Raids era
- **Success factors**: Church community, extended family networks, cultural pride

**Case Study 3 - Contemporary Asian Immigration (2000s)**:
*Li Wei and Zhang Min - Chinese immigrants*

- **Departure**: Education and business opportunities, 2005
- **Journey**: Direct flight from Shanghai to Auckland
- **Settlement**: IT business in North Shore, children in local schools
- **Challenges**: Professional qualification recognition, cultural integration
- **Success factors**: Education, business networks, multicultural environment

**Analysis Questions**:

- What common challenges did all groups face?
- How did government attitudes change over time?
- What role did existing communities play in helping newcomers?
- How did each group contribute to New Zealand society?

---

### 🎭 **Activity 3: Immigration Stories Drama Workshop**

**Time**: 50 minutes | **Materials**: Simple props, character backgrounds, historical context

**Performance Task**: Create and perform short scenes showing immigrant experiences

**Scene Options**:

**Scene 1: "The Decision to Leave"** (10 minutes)

- **Setting**: Home country, family discussion about emigrating
- **Characters**: Family members with different opinions
- **Conflict**: Weighing risks vs. opportunities of moving to New Zealand
- **Cultural context**: Research actual historical reasons for leaving

**Scene 2: "Arrival in Aotearoa"** (10 minutes)

- **Setting**: Port of entry (Wellington, Auckland, Lyttelton)
- **Characters**: New arrivals, immigration officials, existing residents
- **Focus**: First impressions, official processes, immediate challenges
- **Historical accuracy**: Use authentic details from the time period

**Scene 3: "Building Community"** (10 minutes)

- **Setting**: Early settlement - church, school, workplace, neighborhood
- **Characters**: Immigrants and established residents working together
- **Theme**: How communities form and support each other
- **Modern connection**: Compare to how communities form today

**Performance Criteria**:

- **Historical accuracy**: 30% - Use authentic details and context
- **Character development**: 25% - Show realistic immigrant experiences
- **Cultural sensitivity**: 25% - Respectful portrayal of all groups
- **Communication**: 20% - Clear presentation and audience engagement

**Reflection Questions** (After performances):

- What emotions did the characters experience?
- How have immigration experiences changed over time?
- What can we learn from past immigrant experiences?
- How does this connect to current immigration debates?

## Assessment Opportunities

### **Formative Assessment**

- **Think-Pair-Share**: "What challenges might your family have faced if they immigrated to NZ in the 1800s?"
- **Exit Tickets**: "One new thing I learned..." / "One question I still have..."
- **Gallery Walk**: Students view and comment on each other's migration maps

### **Summative Assessment Options**

**Option A - Research Report** (750 words):
Choose one immigrant group and analyze their:

- Reasons for immigration (push/pull factors)
- Settlement patterns and challenges
- Contributions to New Zealand society
- Modern status and continuing connections

**Option B - Museum Exhibition**:
Create a museum display on "Immigration to Our Local Area" featuring:

- Historical timeline with local examples
- Artifact replicas or photos with explanations
- Interactive element (quiz, map, audio recording)
- Connection to contemporary immigration

**Option C - Digital Story**:
Create a 5-minute video/podcast featuring:

- Interview with local immigrant or descendant
- Historical context and background research
- Visual elements (photos, maps, graphics)
- Reflection on how immigration shaped your community

### **Success Indicators**

- **Achieved**: Identifies immigration patterns and basic impacts
- **Merit**: Analyzes multiple perspectives and makes connections
- **Excellence**: Evaluates complex relationships between immigration and social change

## Differentiation Strategies

### **Support for Struggling Learners**

- Provide graphic organizers for comparing immigrant groups
- Use visual timelines and maps before text-heavy resources
- Pair with stronger readers for research tasks
- Offer choice of assessment formats

### **Challenge for Advanced Students**

- Research primary sources (diaries, letters, government records)
- Compare New Zealand immigration to other countries
- Analyze changing immigration policies and their effects
- Lead class discussions and peer teaching opportunities

### **Cultural Responsiveness**

- Include diverse range of immigrant voices and experiences
- Connect to students' own family immigration stories
- Acknowledge Māori as tangata whenua (people of the land)
- Avoid stereotypes and ensure balanced perspectives

## Cultural Safety & Inclusive Practices

### **Respectful Representation**

⚠️ **Essential Guidelines**:

- Avoid stereotypes and generalizations about any group
- Include diverse voices within each immigrant community
- Acknowledge both positive and challenging experiences
- Recognize ongoing impacts of historical policies

### **Māori Perspectives**

- Acknowledge Māori as tangata whenua (indigenous people)
- Understand immigration through indigenous lens
- Recognize impacts of colonization on Māori
- Include Māori responses to different immigration waves

### **Contemporary Connections**

- Link historical immigration to current refugee/immigration issues
- Discuss how past experiences inform present policies
- Celebrate positive contributions while acknowledging challenges
- Encourage empathy and understanding across communities

### **Classroom Environment**

- Create safe spaces for sharing family stories
- Validate all students' cultural backgrounds
- Address any prejudice or discrimination sensitively
- Foster respect for diverse immigration experiences
